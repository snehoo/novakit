The Book Cover Brief Your Designer Actually Needs

Most AI-generated cover briefs describe your story. Designers need visual direction — genre signals, typographic conventions, mood references. Here's why that distinction matters, and what changes when the brief is built by a skill that researches the shelf first.

You've spent months on the manuscript. You know the story inside out — the tone, the themes, the emotional arc. So when you sit down to brief a designer or generate a cover concept, you describe the book. The protagonist. The world. The feeling you want readers to have.

The designer nods. Or the AI generates something. And what comes back looks like it could be any book in a vague adjacent genre — competent, inoffensive, and invisible on a crowded shelf.

The problem isn't the designer. It's the brief. Describing your story is not the same as briefing a cover. A cover brief is a genre document, a visual argument, a positioning statement. It answers questions your story description never touches: What do the top-selling covers in your sub-genre have in common right now? What typographic treatment signals "literary thriller" vs "psychological suspense"? What's the one visual element readers in your category use as a buying signal? Writing that brief from memory — or from a generic AI that doesn't know what's on the shelves this season — is where most self-published authors lose the cover before the design has even started.

Why Describing Your Story Is the Wrong Brief

A cover exists to do one thing before a reader opens it: signal that this book belongs to the category they're browsing, and that it's worth stopping for. That's a genre problem, not a narrative problem. It requires knowledge of visual patterns — the colour palettes dominating dark romance right now, the way cozy mystery covers have shifted from illustrated to photographic in the past two years, the exact font weight that reads as "upmarket fiction" vs "beach read."

None of that information lives in your manuscript. And none of it is something a generic AI session knows with any precision, because visual trends in publishing shift season by season, and what was on-trend eighteen months ago looks dated to a reader who buys three books a week.

💡
The core problem

A cover brief written from your story describes what the book is about. A brief written from genre research describes what the cover needs to communicate — and those are two entirely different documents.

The result of a story-based brief is a cover that looks like it was designed in a vacuum. It might be beautiful. It might perfectly capture the themes. And it will still get scrolled past by readers who are pattern-matching against the genre conventions they've learned to trust — because it doesn't speak the visual language they're looking for.

What Live Genre Research Changes in the Brief

Before the Book Cover Prompt skill writes a single line of your brief, it does something a generic session doesn't: it researches the current visual landscape of your genre. What are the bestselling covers in your sub-category doing right now? What colour treatments are dominant? What's being overused — and therefore worth avoiding to stand out? What typographic styles signal your sub-genre to a reader scrolling at speed?

That research becomes the foundation of your brief. Not the story — the shelf. The skill then folds your specific narrative elements in on top: the tone, the key visual, the protagonist if they're featured, the mood reference you want to hit. But it does that framing work inside a genre context, not in spite of it.

A brief that starts with the genre tells your designer what the cover has to earn before it can afford to be distinctive.

The practical difference shows up immediately. Instead of "I want something dark and atmospheric with a sense of mystery," your brief specifies the colour temperature range dominating psychological thrillers this cycle, the font treatment that signals the sub-genre, the specific type of imagery — silhouette, object, environmental — that's currently converting in your category, and the one place your cover should deliberately break from convention to create visual tension. That's a brief a designer can execute with confidence. It's also a brief that produces a cover readers in your genre will instinctively trust.

✓ With NovaKit Skill
Genre: Domestic noir / psychological thriller.

Visual direction: Current bestsellers in this sub-genre favour close-cropped environmental imagery over full silhouettes — a domestic object (door handle, wine glass, window reflection) carrying visual dread outperforms a posed figure at this price point.

Colour palette: Desaturated teal-to-charcoal gradient, with a single warm accent (amber or burnt sienna) to create tension. Avoid pure black backgrounds — they're reading as self-published in this category right now.

Typography: High-contrast serif title, condensed weight, off-white. Author name in a lighter sans-serif below. No script fonts.

Differentiation point: A reflection or partial reveal — something in the image that suggests the reader isn't seeing the full picture. This is the "hook" that earns the second look on a crowded shelf.

The second brief answers every question a designer needs before they open their software: what to put in frame, what colour logic to follow, what typographic treatment signals the sub-genre, and what visual move creates stopping power.
