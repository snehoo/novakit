Generic AI Gives Every Brand the Same Brand

Type "create a brand identity for my wellness coaching business" into a vanilla AI session and you'll get something like this: sage green or warm terracotta, a thin serif typeface, clean white space, soft rounded edges, messaging about transformation and authenticity. It will be well-constructed. It will look professional. It will also look identical to the wellness brand that launched six weeks ago, and the one that launched six months before that.

This isn't a failure of intelligence. It's a failure of input. When you ask a generic AI for a brand identity with no competitive context and no positioning constraints, it produces the statistically central answer for your category. The most common colour. The most common type pairing. The visual shorthand that says "I am a professional in this space" — which is precisely the shorthand everyone else in your space is using.

Differentiation, by definition, requires knowing what you're differentiating from. That knowledge isn't in a generic prompt. It has to be built in.

Why AI Defaults to Category Conventions

Here's what's happening under the surface. A language model's idea of "what a wellness brand looks like" is built from everything it's been trained on — thousands of wellness brand descriptions, brand guidelines, mood boards, marketing copy, and visual identity documents. The pattern that emerges from that training is the consensus: the most frequently validated combination of choices across all of that material.

When you ask for a brand identity without specifying that you want to break from those conventions, the model produces the consensus. It's not being lazy. It's being accurate to the task as given. "Brand identity for a wellness coaching business" is a request for something that reads as a wellness coaching business — and that's exactly what you get.

💡
The actual problem

An AI asked to create a brand identity without competitive context optimises for recognisability within a category. That's the opposite of what most brands need — which is distinction from the category, not membership in it.

The same logic applies to every component of a brand identity. Typography defaults to the most legible, most professional, most commonly used pairing for the category. Colour defaults to the palette that signals trustworthiness or approachability or energy — depending on which of those adjectives you used. The result is a brand that communicates its category clearly and its specific positioning not at all.

What Calibration Actually Changes

The Logo & Brand Identity skill runs a different process before touching any visual recommendation. It researches the current visual landscape of your specific market — not your industry in general, but the real competitive set you're operating in. Which palettes are overrepresented. Which typographic conventions have become category clichés. Where visual sameness has created a gap that distinction can occupy.

That research changes the entire framing of what the brief recommends. Instead of "here are colours that communicate trustworthiness," the output becomes "here are colours that communicate trustworthiness while standing out from the three dominant visual conventions currently crowding your market." Those are different briefs that produce different logos.

The question isn't what looks good for your category. It's what looks different from everything already in it.

The three-question calibration process is also doing specific work here. It doesn't ask what your brand should look like. It asks who your audience is, what position you want to own in the market, and what the one thing is that makes your offering different from the alternatives. Those answers constrain the brief in a way that breaks it out of the category default and anchors every visual recommendation to something specific about your business.

The Comparison — Same Business, Two Different Inputs

Here's a concrete example. A B2B SaaS company selling project management software for architecture firms. Small team, niche market, founder-led sales. They want a brand identity.

Vanilla Claude — no calibration
For a B2B SaaS in the AEC space, I'd recommend a professional, modern identity. Consider a deep navy or slate blue as your primary colour — these convey reliability and expertise. Pair with a clean sans-serif like a geometric typeface for a contemporary feel. The logo could be a wordmark with a simple architectural icon (a grid, a line drawing of a structure). White space is important. Messaging should emphasise efficiency and professional credibility.
✓ With NovaKit Skill — calibrated brief
Market context: AEC software space is visually dominated by navy/slate + geometric sans combinations (Autodesk, Procore, PlanGrid). Any brand entering this space with that palette reads as a commodity player.

Differentiation direction: Warm charcoal primary with a raw amber accent — signals precision without the corporate coldness that characterises the dominant players. Architects respond to materials references; the palette should feel like a drawing table, not a dashboard.

Type: A humanist mono for the wordmark (structural, technical, but warm) paired with a transitional serif for marketing copy — creates an editorial quality that no direct competitor currently owns.

Avoid entirely: blue in any form, geometric icon treatments, anything that references construction or buildings directly.

The vanilla output isn't wrong — navy and sans-serif are professionally appropriate for this space. But "professionally appropriate for this space" is exactly the problem. The calibrated brief gives the designer something to differentiate against, not just something to execute. The difference between those two briefs is the difference between a brand that blends into the category and one that stands out of it.

What the Category-Saturated Output Actually Costs You

A brand identity that defaults to category conventions isn't free. The logo gets built. The colours get applied. The brand launches. Six months later, someone who works with your target customers mentions that your brand looks like several other companies they've seen recently — and they can't quite place what makes yours different.

That's not a trivial problem for a founder-led business or a freelancer building a personal brand. When differentiation isn't visible in the identity, it has to be carried entirely by the messaging — and most messaging isn't strong enough to do that job alone. The visual identity either reinforces the positioning or undermines it. There's no neutral.

The comparison table below shows where generic AI brand briefs fall short at each decision point, and what a calibrated brief accounts for instead:

Decision point Generic AI brief Calibrated brand brief
Colour palette Chosen for category association ("green = finance") Chosen relative to what competitors are using — avoids saturated palettes
Typography Most legible / most common pairing for the category Chosen to create distinction from the 2–3 dominant type conventions in the market
Logo form Whatever is most associated with the industry Chosen to signal category membership while breaking from the visual cliché
Brand personality Adjectives ("bold, modern, trustworthy") Behavioural — how the brand acts in every touchpoint, relative to how competitors act
Differentiation Not addressed — output optimises for category fit Central to every recommendation — every choice made against the competitive backdrop

Who This Matters Most For

The situation where this matters most

Any founder or freelancer entering a market that already has an established visual language — fintech, wellness, SaaS, legal services, consulting — where the default AI output will produce something that looks like the category, not something that stands out of it.

The problem is sharpest in crowded markets where brand identity does real commercial work. When your potential clients are choosing between three similar-looking options, the one that looks distinctly different gets more consideration — not because people are consciously evaluating visual identities, but because differentiation signals confidence. A brand that deliberately doesn't look like everyone else is implicitly claiming it doesn't need to.

If you're building in a space where all the competitors already look the same, that's not a constraint. That's an opening. You just need a brief that knows it's there.

Generic AI doesn't produce bad brand identities. It produces median ones. And in a market full of brands all optimising for the same category signals, median is invisible. The skill doesn't guarantee a remarkable brand — that still takes creative execution. But it gives any designer, AI or human, a brief that knows the difference between what looks right for the category and what looks right for you specifically. That's not a small thing. That's where the work actually starts.
