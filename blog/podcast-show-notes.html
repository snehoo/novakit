Your Show Notes Are a Webpage Nobody Finds

Most podcasters write show notes as episode summaries — a few sentences about what was covered, a guest bio, some links. That document is invisible to search, unconvincing to cold visitors, and a missed opportunity with every guest you've hosted. Here's what the skill builds instead.

Every episode you publish creates a webpage. That webpage sits on your domain, accumulates whatever authority your site has built, and is theoretically accessible to anyone searching for the topics you covered. For most podcasters, "theoretically" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. The page exists but earns no traffic, because the content on it — a paragraph summary, a guest bio, a list of links — contains none of the language people actually use when they search for information on the episode's subject.

Show notes written as summaries describe what happened in the conversation. Show notes built for search answer the questions the conversation addressed — in the specific phrasing someone would use to find those answers. Those are different documents. The first one works for existing listeners who already know they want the episode. The second one works for everyone who doesn't know your podcast exists yet but is actively searching for exactly what your guest spent forty minutes explaining.

Most podcasters never build the second document. Not because they don't want the search traffic, but because writing it properly — extracting the key insights, restructuring them around searchable questions, writing meta copy that earns clicks from results pages, building timestamp navigation that keeps visitors on the page — takes longer than recording the episode. So the summary goes up, the page gets ignored by search, and the episode reaches only the audience that was already there.

What Show Notes Are Actually For

A well-built show notes page serves four distinct jobs simultaneously — and most podcast show notes accomplish none of them well.

Search discovery. The episode page is the only text-crawlable version of your audio content. Google cannot index what your guest said. It can index what you wrote about it. A page with 800 words of structured, keyword-relevant content around a guest's expertise will rank for searches your existing audience is never making — the searches that bring new listeners who discover your show through the topic, not through the show itself.

Cold visitor conversion. Someone who lands on a show notes page from search has never heard your podcast. They're evaluating whether the episode is worth an hour of their time based entirely on what they can read in thirty seconds. A summary paragraph tells them what was discussed. A well-structured notes page with key takeaways, quotable moments, and a clear argument for why this episode matters converts them from visitor to listener.

Guest relationship capital. Every guest you host has an audience. A notes page that showcases their expertise clearly — well-structured, searchable, professionally presented — is something they'll link to, share, and reference. A summary paragraph is not. The quality of the show notes page is often the difference between a guest who mentions the episode once and a guest who shares it repeatedly because it reflects well on them every time someone reads it.

Evergreen asset building. A podcast episode is perceived as time-sensitive — listeners feel behind if they haven't heard it. A show notes page framed around the expertise rather than the episode date lives indefinitely. An episode from two years ago about SEO strategy that has a well-optimised notes page will continue bringing in search traffic long after it's stopped appearing in new-episode feeds.

The core insight

Show notes are not episode documentation. They're a standalone piece of content that needs to work for someone who has never heard your podcast and is deciding whether to start. Writing them as a summary serves your existing listeners. Writing them as a content asset serves your growth.

What Episode Research Changes About the Output

The NovaKit Podcast Show Notes skill doesn't start by summarising. Before any copy is written, the skill researches the search landscape around the episode's core topic — the questions people are actually asking in this subject area, the keyword phrases that have real search volume, the related topics that a well-structured notes page could rank for alongside the primary subject. That research shapes the structure, the headings, the key takeaway framing, and the meta copy — so the page is built to be found, not just to exist.

A show notes page built for search is written for the listener who hasn't found you yet. A summary is written for the one who already has.

The skill also calibrates to the episode's specific content and guest. A technical interview with a software engineer produces different notes than a narrative conversation with a founder — different key insight structures, different quotable moment framing, different timestamp organisation. The output isn't a generic notes template filled in with episode details. It's a document shaped by what actually happened in the conversation and what a cold visitor landing on the page needs to understand about it to decide to listen.

Summary Notes vs. Skill-Built Notes

Same episode: an interview with a growth marketing consultant on why most SaaS companies misread their churn data. Here's the opening section of a generic summary versus the skill-built version:

We also discuss common mistakes companies make when reading their data and what to do instead.

Guest links: janesmithconsulting.com | @janesmith
✓ Skill-built notes — search and conversion structured
Why Your Churn Rate Is Lying to You — with Jane Smith

Most SaaS teams track the wrong churn number — and the one they're optimising for is actively masking the cohort behaviour that predicts collapse twelve months out. Jane Smith has spent a decade inside growth teams at Series A through Series C companies untangling this exact problem. This episode is the diagnosis most founders don't get until it's expensive.

What you'll take away: The three churn metrics that mislead, the cohort view that reveals what's actually happening, and the retention intervention that works at under 500 customers but breaks above it.

The generic version describes the conversation and introduces the guest. The skill-built version opens with the central claim — "your churn rate is lying to you" — and immediately frames why it matters to the reader, not just what was discussed. The SEO title targets the search intent ("why churn rate is misleading") rather than the episode label. The "what you'll take away" line gives a cold visitor the specific reason to commit an hour of their time. The guest is introduced through what she knows, not just who she is.
