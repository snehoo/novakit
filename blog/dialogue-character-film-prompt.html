Ask any AI to write a scene between a detective and a suspect, and you'll get the same thing: the detective asks direct questions, the suspect deflects, someone says something ominous in the last line. The prose is clean. The structure is correct. And it reads like every other AI-generated interrogation scene on the internet.

The problem isn't that the AI can't write dialogue. It's that it has no idea who these two people are before the conversation starts. No relationship history. No unspoken objective behind each line. No sense of what this scene costs each character emotionally. So it defaults to the most statistically probable version of an interrogation scene — which is exactly what a hundred other scripts already look like.

The Dialogue & Character Film Prompt skill starts by fixing that. Before a single line of dialogue is written, it establishes the psychological architecture of every character in the scene. What they want. What they're hiding. What they're afraid to say directly. That context doesn't appear in the output — it shapes the output.

Why Vanilla Claude Produces Placeholder Characters

Generic AI dialogue fails because it optimises for surface plausibility. The lines are grammatically correct, the genre signals are in the right places, and the scene advances as expected. What's missing is the thing that makes any line of dialogue memorable: a character saying something that only they would say, in the way they would say it, because of who they specifically are.

A grieving father doesn't talk like a grieving father in the abstract. He talks like a man who hasn't slept in four days, who blames himself in ways he hasn't admitted to anyone, who has a habit of making dark jokes when he's cornered. When vanilla Claude writes him, it writes the archetype. When a skill builds his psychology first, it writes the person.

💡
The core problem

AI writes the version of your character that appears most often in training data. The Dialogue & Character Film Prompt skill writes the version that exists only in your script.

This is what directors mean when they say a script reads flat on the page. Not flat in terms of action — flat in terms of specificity. Every line could have been said by a different character in a different film, and the scene would still technically work. That's not a script. That's scaffolding.

What Character Research Looks Like Before the Scene

The skill doesn't ask you to write a character bible before it can run. It interviews you — three focused questions that extract the psychological and relational context the scene needs. Who each character is trying to be in this scene versus who they actually are. What the scene is doing for the story structurally, and what it costs the characters personally. What the genre requires of the pacing and what subtext should be running under the spoken lines.

That's the calibration. It takes two minutes. And it's the difference between a scene that could belong to any script and one that belongs to yours specifically.

Dialogue is what a character says. Subtext is what they mean. The skill writes both — and makes sure they're never the same thing.

The skill also reads current screenplay structure patterns before drafting — not the conventions from five years ago that most AI models were trained on. What's landing in contemporary festival shorts, what pacing structures are being rewarded in streaming pilots, how dialogue-driven scenes are being cut differently now than they were in traditional three-act structures. That context feeds directly into the rhythm and density of the scene it produces.

How the Dialogue & Character Skill Works

Here's what runs before you see any dialogue on the page.

1
Character psychology mapping
Each character in the scene gets a psychological profile: their surface objective (what they say they want), their actual objective (what they're really after), and their vulnerability (what they can't afford to reveal). These three layers generate the tension between what characters say and what they mean.
2
Relationship dynamic calibration
Two strangers talk differently than two people who've known each other for twenty years and stopped trusting each other six months ago. The skill maps the power dynamic, the history, and the emotional stakes between characters before the first line is written.
3
Genre and tone alignment
A tense thriller scene and a darkly comic scene can share the same setup and produce completely different dialogue. The skill reads the genre requirements and adjusts pacing, line length, interruption frequency, and subtext density accordingly.
4
Scene function identification
Every scene in a well-structured script does at least two things simultaneously. The skill identifies the scene's functional role — what it reveals, what it changes, what it sets up — and embeds both functions into the dialogue structure rather than just one.
5
Anti-slop quality gate
Before the scene is delivered, an automatic quality pass checks for: generic opening lines, exposition delivered as dialogue, characters explaining things they would already know, and endings that summarise rather than turn. Anything that fails gets rewritten before you see it.

Generic Output vs Calibrated Output

Same scene setup: a mother confronts her adult son about a lie he told three years ago. Here's what each version looks like.

Without NovaKit

DANIEL
Mom, I don't know what you're talking about.

SARAH
Don't lie to me. I found out the truth. Everything you told me back then was a lie.

DANIEL
Okay. Fine. I lied. But I did it to protect you. You have to understand that.
✓ With NovaKit Skill
SARAH
I made your father's pasta last night. First time since the funeral. I don't know why I told you that.

Daniel doesn't say anything. He knows.

SARAH (CONT'D)
The thing is, I keep trying to remember who told me it was a car accident. And I can't figure out if it was you or if I just decided it myself.

DANIEL
Mom —

SARAH
I'm not asking you a question.

The second version doesn't announce the conflict. It arrives sideways — through a pasta detail that carries the whole weight of grief — and lands in a line that does three things at once: stops Daniel from deflecting, reveals that Sarah has been thinking about this for a long time, and signals that this conversation is not going to go the way he planned. That's what character psychology and subtext architecture produce when the skill has done its groundwork.
