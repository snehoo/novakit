You paste in a concept — two characters, a setting, a rough idea of what needs to happen — and Claude gives you something that looks like a screenplay. The INT./EXT. headers are there. The action lines are present tense and reasonably lean. The dialogue is spaced correctly. You read through it and feel, briefly, like you have something.

Then you try to work with it. The character who was established as withdrawn in scene one is suddenly cracking jokes in scene three with no bridge between. The act break lands in the wrong place — the tension peaks early and then flatters out before the climax. The dialogue is grammatically fine but sounds like no one you've ever met. You can't shoot this. You can barely revise it, because fixing one scene pulls threads that run through four others.

This is the specific failure mode of generic AI in screenplay writing: it produces structure without story. It knows what a script looks like but doesn't understand what a script does. What changes things is bringing actual craft knowledge into the generation process — knowing what's working in the genre right now, what structure the story's concept demands, and how the specific characters you've described actually speak.

Why Script Format Fools You Into Thinking You Have a Draft

Screenplay format is highly standardised. One-inch margins, Courier 12pt, sluglines in a specific style, action blocks written in present tense, character names centred above dialogue. Generic AI learned all of this from the vast amount of correctly-formatted script content on the internet. It formats impeccably.

That precision creates a problem. A page of well-formatted screenplay text reads, at first glance, like professional work. The visual structure of the medium — the white space, the clean blocks, the familiar layout — carries an authority that a page of raw prose doesn't. You're much more likely to read through a badly-formatted short story and notice it's bad than to read through a correctly-formatted but dramatically inert screenplay and notice the same thing.

💡
The core problem

Format compliance is table stakes — it tells you nothing about whether the script actually works. A screenplay that looks professional and reads flat will get the same coverage note every time: "Pass — competent execution, no emotional engine."

The problems in a generic AI script are structural and below the surface. Scene transitions exist but don't build. Characters speak in ways that reveal information to the audience rather than in ways those particular people would actually speak. The climactic moment arrives, technically, in the right place — but there's no weight behind it because the preceding scenes didn't earn it. These aren't typos you can fix. They're architectural.

What Live Research Changes for a Script Skill

A screenplay isn't an eternal form that exists outside of time and market. Every genre has conventions that shift — what's reading as fresh versus tired, what structural approaches are getting traction with development executives, which dialogue styles feel contemporary versus which ones mark the writer as working from a 2015 mental model of what good scenes look like. A skill built to research before it writes goes and finds this out. Not from training data. From what's current.

That research shapes the output in ways that are specific rather than decorative. A thriller concept in 2026 gets treated differently from the same concept in a skill that learned from scripts written ten years ago — because the pacing expectations, the character complexity readers want, and the types of twists that still land have all shifted. Generic AI doesn't know this. It averages across everything it was trained on and produces something that could have been written in any year.

The skill that researches before it writes knows what the genre is doing right now — not what it was doing when the training data was assembled.

The calibration also extends to production context. A short film script for a micro-budget shoot has different requirements than a feature spec that needs to function as a writing sample. A corporate video script needs to deliver a specific message inside a specific runtime. A pilot needs to establish a world and leave room for the series — which is a completely different structural problem than a standalone screenplay. The Screenplay / Script skill asks where you are before it writes — and the output reflects that, specifically.

What the Screenplay / Script Skill Actually Does

The skill opens with three calibrating questions that define the output before a single line is written. Genre, production context, and character intent — these aren't decorative. They're the variables that determine the entire structural approach. Here's how it works in practice:

Context calibration before generation
The skill asks about your format (feature, short, pilot, corporate), your genre, and the production stage you're at (concept development, first draft, revision pass). The same story concept produces fundamentally different script output depending on these answers — a first-draft feature gets a full three-act structure; a short film gets tighter pacing and a compressed arc; a corporate video gets message-led structure with clear call-to-action beats.
2
Live genre and format research
Before writing, the skill researches what's currently working in the genre — structural patterns getting traction, dialogue styles that read as contemporary, pacing conventions that match current audience expectations. This is what prevents the output from feeling like it was assembled from a training corpus rather than written with awareness of the craft as it's practised now.
3
Character-voice differentiation
Characters are given distinct speech patterns based on the context you provide — not just personality adjectives but actual linguistic markers: sentence length, vocabulary range, what they interrupt versus what they let pass. Two characters in the same scene sound like different people because the skill built their voices from the information you gave it, not from a generic "character dialogue" template.
4
Anti-slop quality pass before delivery
A quality check runs automatically before you see the output. Flat scene openings, on-the-nose exposition, dialogue that exists only to inform the audience — these get rewritten. The output you receive has already passed through a craft filter. You're not handed the first draft and left to find the problems yourself.
