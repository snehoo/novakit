How to Write a Real Estate Listing Description That Actually Sells the Property

Not a list of adjectives to avoid — the structural rules behind listing copy that makes the right buyer stop scrolling, read everything, and book a viewing before the weekend.

You know your home better than anyone else ever will. You know that the kitchen is at its best at six in the evening when the light comes through the west-facing window and hits the worktops in a way the photos can't capture. You know the neighbour who keeps the shared path spotless. You know that the walk to the station takes eleven minutes at a normal pace and that there's a shortcut through the park that makes it nine. You know which room your children fought over and why.

None of that knowledge is in most listing descriptions. Instead, most self-written listings read like a room-by-room inventory: entrance hall, reception room, kitchen-diner, three bedrooms, family bathroom, rear garden. Factually correct, emotionally inert. The buyer scrolls past to look at the photos, makes a quick calculation about whether the price-per-square-foot is right, and moves on. The copy that should have been doing selling work did none.

Here are the structural rules behind listing copy that actually moves people — not tips about avoiding clichés, but the framework that changes what the description does and who it stops.

The Six Rules of Listing Copy That Converts

1
Decide who you're writing for before you write a word
Every property has a most likely buyer — not the only buyer, but the person for whom this property is the closest to ideal given their current life situation. A two-bedroom flat near a university district is most likely selling to a young professional couple or a parent buying for a student child. A five-bedroom detached with a double garage is most likely selling to a family with teenagers or a couple who entertain frequently. Identify that person specifically before you write the first sentence, and write the entire description for them. Everything else — the features you lead with, the language you use, what you omit — follows from that decision.
Instead of writing for everyone
"Ideal for a variety of buyers including families, professionals, and investors."
Pick one. Write for them. The others will still read it — and they'll find it more compelling because it feels specific.
2
Open with the life, not the property
The first sentence of your listing is the only one guaranteed to be read. It determines whether the buyer reads the second sentence or moves on. Most first sentences describe a property type: "A beautifully presented three-bedroom semi-detached family home." This contains no information the buyer doesn't already know from the listing header. A first sentence that opens on the life the property enables — the morning routine, the thing that makes the location specifically right, the quality that makes this house different from the eleven others they looked at this week — earns the second sentence.
Compare these opening lines
"A beautifully presented three-bedroom property in the sought-after village of Thornton."
"Thornton is the kind of village where the primary school headteacher knows the children by name — which is usually what ends the search for families who've been looking for six months."
3
Translate every feature into the life it enables
A feature is a fact. The life it enables is the sell. South-facing garden is a fact. The place where Sunday mornings happen from March to October is the sell. Open-plan kitchen-diner is a fact. The layout that means the person cooking isn't separated from the conversation is the sell. For every feature you're tempted to list, ask: what does this actually allow the buyer to do that they can't do without it? Write that instead of the feature — or write both, with the feature as the evidence and the life as the lead.
Feature vs. life
"The property benefits from a home office / study."
"The study is at the far end of the house — quiet enough to take calls without closing the door, with its own access to the garden for the mid-afternoon break that makes the day work."
4
Be specific about the neighbourhood, not general
Location copy is almost always generic: "within easy reach of local amenities," "good transport links," "well-regarded local schools." These phrases contain no information the buyer couldn't infer from looking at a map. Specific location copy — the café that opens at 7am, the twelve-minute walk to the station, the school that's consistently oversubscribed — is searchable, verifiable, and signals that someone who actually lives here wrote this. Buyers who are new to an area especially rely on this detail. Give them the specific things you'd tell a friend who was moving in next door.
General vs. specific location
"Conveniently located close to local amenities and transport links."
"The 7:42 from Thornton reaches Waterloo in 34 minutes. The bakery on the high street opens at 7am and sells out of sourdough by 9. Both matter more than you'd expect."
5
Cut the adjectives that have lost all meaning
There is a set of words so overused in property listing copy that buyers' eyes now pass over them without registering any content. They are not wrong exactly — your home probably is spacious, stunning, and rarely available. But they register as filler rather than description because they appear in virtually every listing at every price point in every location. Every word you spend on them is a word you're not spending on something specific, true, and differentiating.
stunning spacious must-see rarely available sought-after ideal for entertaining beautifully presented deceptively spacious early viewing advised chain-free opportunity not to be missed
Replace each one with something observable: not "spacious kitchen" but "wide enough for two people to cook in at the same time without negotiating." Not "stunning views" but "the bedroom window faces east — the sunrise is the thing visitors always comment on."
6
End with a reason to act, not a summary
Most listing descriptions end with either a room-by-room summary or a generic call to action: "internal viewing is highly recommended." The close of your listing is the last thing the buyer reads before deciding whether to save the property, share it with their partner, or book a viewing. It should give them a specific reason to do that today rather than coming back to it later — a detail about the property that only becomes fully clear at a viewing, or a fact about the current market position that creates genuine urgency without being manipulative about it.
Generic close vs. earned close
"Internal viewing is highly recommended to fully appreciate this exceptional property."
"The garden is the thing the photos don't do justice. Book a viewing for late afternoon if you can — you'll understand why the previous owners never moved."

The Part Only You Can Write

These six rules produce the architecture. They don't produce the content — that comes from knowing the property the way only the person who has lived in it or sold dozens like it can know it. The specific detail that makes your listing different from every other three-bedroom semi in the same postcode is knowledge you already have and that no template or skill can supply for you.

🔑
Before you write, answer these

What is the thing about this property that you'd tell a close friend first? What did you use the garden / study / kitchen for that you'll miss? Which room surprised you when you first viewed it? What's within walking distance that doesn't show up on a map? What kind of person would be happiest here — and why is this the right house for that person specifically?

Those answers are your raw material. The six rules above are the framework you build them into. The first draft written with both — your specific knowledge shaped by the structural rules — will be more compelling than anything produced by someone who hasn't been inside the property, however skilled a writer they are.

The detail that closes the sale is almost always something only the seller knew to include. The framework just makes sure it's in the right place.

A Final Note on Refreshing a Listing That Isn't Moving

If your property has been on the market for more than four weeks without offers, the copy is almost always worth revisiting before the price. A listing that was written quickly at the point of going live often hasn't been reconsidered since — and the buyer persona it was written for may not be the buyer who is actually viewing and not proceeding. The viewings tell you something the listing didn't ask: who is responding to this property, and is the copy speaking to them or to someone else?

✓
The refresh test

Read your current listing as if you are the buyer who viewed last weekend and didn't make an offer. Does it speak to the life they were imagining? Does the opening line reflect what they actually responded to when they walked through? Does it include the specific detail that made them linger in one room longer than the others? If not, the copy is the diagnosis — and refreshing it costs nothing compared to reducing the asking price.

A listing refresh written with these rules often produces a meaningfully different document from what went live the first time — because the first version was written before you knew which buyers were interested and what they were responding to. The second version can be written with that knowledge. It's a different brief, and it tends to produce different results.
