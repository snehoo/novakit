The Vows That Sound Like You Wrote Them — Because You Did

Generic AI wedding vows have the right structure, the right length, and the wrong everything else. Here's what changes when the skill learns about your partner before it writes a single word about them.

You know exactly what you want to say. You know the moment you realised this was the person. You know the specific thing they do — the way they handle bad news, the thing they say when you're anxious, the look they gave you the first time you saw them properly — and you know that whatever you say on the day has to contain that, or it isn't really your vows. It's just vows.

The problem isn't knowing what to say. The problem is that when you sit down to write it, what comes out reads like a wedding speech from a film about a wedding. "From the moment I met you, I knew." "You make me a better person." "I promise to love you through every storm." You've heard all of it before. You're going to say it in front of everyone you know. And somewhere between knowing exactly how you feel and the blank page, the specific, true thing you wanted to say turned into a greeting card.

This is the gap the Wedding Vows Writer skill is built to close. Not by writing vows for you — by writing vows from you, from the actual details you give it about your actual person.

What Generic AI Vows Actually Produce

Ask Claude — without calibration, without any context about your relationship — to write wedding vows, and you'll get something structurally sound. An opening about when you knew. A middle section about specific qualities, described in general terms. A set of promises, each one beginning with "I promise to." A closing about forever. It's three minutes long. It hits the expected beats. It will make your aunt cry because weddings make your aunt cry, not because anything in it is specific enough to land differently on anyone who knows you both.

The tell is in the details — or the absence of them. Generic vows contain no details that could only come from your relationship. They contain no moment that your partner will recognise and feel seen by. They contain no specific promise that means something because of a specific history. The word "adventures" appears. The phrase "your laugh" appears. Nothing in it is wrong. Nothing in it is yours.

💡
The core problem

Vows written without knowing anything about your partner are vows about love in general — which is the one thing your vows are specifically not supposed to be about. Specificity is what separates vows from a speech at a stranger's wedding.

You can feel the difference when you read them back. Generic vows produce a vague sense of satisfaction — they say the right things — followed by an equally vague sense that you still haven't written your vows. Because you haven't. You've produced a structure that needs to be filled with the actual content, which is the specific true thing you knew before you started and still haven't managed to put into sentences.

Why Knowing Your Partner First Changes Everything

The Wedding Vows Writer skill doesn't start writing until it knows something real. Before it produces a draft, it asks you about the moment, the quality, the specific thing — the detail that, if you read the vows back, would make your partner know without any doubt that these were written about them and not slotted into a template with their name in the gaps.

That research changes the output at the sentence level. "You make me feel safe" becomes the specific thing your partner actually does that makes you feel safe — the text they send at midnight, the way they stay quiet when you need to think, the decision they made that one time that told you something important about who they are. Generic AI can't write that sentence because it doesn't know that thing. The skill asks for it first, then builds the vows around it.

The vow that makes someone cry isn't the one about love — it's the one that names a specific moment they thought only the two of you remembered.

The tone calibration matters just as much. Vows that are earnest and sincere read completely differently from vows that are warm and gently funny, which read completely differently from vows that are spare and direct. The skill asks about your register before it writes — because vows that don't sound like you are almost as uncomfortable as vows that don't mention your partner specifically. The person standing across from you on the day knows how you talk. They'll know immediately if the words aren't yours.

What the Wedding Vows Writer Skill Actually Does

The skill gathers three things before writing anything: a moment or story that captures something essential about your partner, the tone you want (sincere, warm and funny, simple and direct, or any combination), and any specific promises or commitments you already know you want to include. Those three inputs are the difference between vows that could have been written for anyone and vows that could only have been written for this person, by you.

1
Story and Detail Gathering
The skill asks for the real material — a specific memory, a quality you noticed early, a moment that clarified something. These become the anchors the vows are built around, so the final draft contains details that are recognisably true rather than recognisably generic.
2
Tone and Voice Calibration
Sincere and earnest, warm with moments of humour, spare and direct — the skill matches your natural register so the vows sound like you at your most considered, not like a professional speechwriter's idea of what wedding vows should sound like.
3
Promise and Commitment Weaving
Any specific promises you already have in mind — the ones that come from your actual life together rather than generic vow templates — are worked into the structure naturally rather than listed as a separate section. Promises that mean something because of a shared history land differently than promises that could apply to anyone.
4
Length and Ceremony Format Matching
The skill calibrates to your ceremony format — religious or civil, traditional or informal, two minutes or four — so the pacing and length fit the actual moment rather than running short or reading like a speech that escaped its container.

The feeling being expressed is identical in both versions: the moment of certainty — the point where you knew this was the person. Here's how the same emotion reads when it's generic versus when it's built from a specific detail.

Today, in front of everyone we love, I choose you.

I knew on the drive home from that dinner in February — the one where your car broke down and we sat on the kerb for two hours waiting and you just started pointing out constellations like it was the obvious thing to do. Your partner hears the right-hand version and knows, in the middle of a room full of people, that it was written about them. That's the difference specificity makes. And specificity only arrives when the skill knows something real before it starts writing.
