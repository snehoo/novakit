A SaaS founder raised $2.1M, wrote a press release, distributed it through a wire service, and waited. Nothing ran. Three weeks later she found a piece in a fintech newsletter covering a competitor's $800K raise — a smaller round, a less differentiated product — with two paragraphs of analysis about what it signalled for B2B payments infrastructure. She read it twice, then read her own press release, and understood immediately what had gone wrong. Her release announced that she had raised money. The piece that ran told a journalist's readers something useful about where the market was going.

This is the failure mode that nearly every self-written press release falls into — not because the company doesn't have a real story, but because the release is written from the inside out. It starts with what the company finds exciting, builds toward the announcement, and ends with a quote from the CEO that could have been drafted by anyone in the building. A journalist reading it has to do all the work of finding the angle, connecting it to what they cover, and deciding whether their readers would care. Most don't do that work. They move to the next email.

The release that gets coverage starts from the journalist's perspective and works backward. It opens with the news hook — the specific, timely reason this matters to this publication's readers — and gives the journalist the context, the quote, and the framing they need to file the piece. Their job becomes easier. That's the only press release worth sending.

Why Generic AI Press Releases Read Like Corporate Announcements

Ask vanilla Claude to write a press release and it produces something structurally correct: headline, dateline, lead paragraph, supporting detail, boilerplate, contact information. The format is right. The language is clean and professional. Every sentence is technically accurate. And the whole thing reads like an internal comms document dressed in press release clothing.

Generic AI doesn't know what TechCrunch is covering this quarter versus what it covered last year. It doesn't know that the beat reporter you're targeting just published three pieces on AI infrastructure consolidation and is actively looking for angles on how smaller players are positioning. It doesn't know that your "strategic partnership" announcement is only interesting to that reporter if it's framed as evidence of a trend they've been tracking, not as a standalone company milestone. So it writes a neutral document that announces facts — and a journalist reading neutral facts about an unfamiliar company has no obvious reason to file anything.

💡
The core problem

A press release written from inside the company announces what happened. A press release written for a specific journalist's beat explains why it matters to their readers right now — and that distinction is the entire difference between coverage and silence.

The quote problem is a particular symptom. Generic press releases produce CEO quotes like "We're thrilled to announce our partnership with X, which represents a major milestone in our mission to transform Y." That quote tells the journalist nothing they couldn't have inferred from the headline, adds no external context, and signals immediately that the release was written by someone inside the company who doesn't understand what a journalist needs from a quote. A usable quote gives the journalist something to build a sentence around — an opinion, a market observation, a specific prediction. Something that couldn't have appeared in the headline because it requires a human voice.

What Live Beat Research Does to a Press Release

Before the PR Press Release skill writes a headline or a lead, it researches what the target publication and beat have been covering recently — which angles are active, which narratives the journalist has been building across their last several pieces, what kind of story would slot naturally into their current editorial direction. This isn't a search for what press releases look like. It's a search for what that journalist's beat looks like this week, so the release can be positioned as the next chapter in a story they're already telling rather than an interruption from an unfamiliar company.

This is the thing a static press release template fundamentally cannot do — and the thing that separates a PR agency's work from a generic document. A good PR professional knows before they write the release which journalists are most likely to cover it and what each of them has been working on. The skill replicates that research step, which means the release that comes out the other end is shaped by actual, current editorial context rather than a general sense of what press releases are supposed to contain.

The best press releases aren't documents. They're research memos for journalists — doing the beat work the journalist would have to do themselves, delivered in the format they need to file.

What that research changes concretely: the news hook in the lead paragraph is positioned relative to what the journalist has been covering, not relative to what the company finds most exciting. The supporting context is drawn from the narrative the beat has been building. The quote is shaped to give the journalist something they can actually use — an opinion or observation that connects the company's news to a broader trend their readers care about. The boilerplate strips out the corporate language that signals "this wasn't written for you" and replaces it with the specific, factual description that makes the company easy to characterise in a single sentence.

What the PR Press Release Skill Actually Does

The skill runs a calibration sequence before writing — four inputs that replace generic press release logic with beat-specific, publication-aware direction.

1
News type and announcement context
Funding round, product launch, partnership, hire, award, research publication, or milestone. Each type has a different news hook logic — a funding announcement is only interesting to most journalists if it signals something about market momentum or investor confidence, not if it just states an amount. The skill adjusts the lead paragraph structure based on news type before writing the first word.
2
Target publication and beat
The publication, the journalist's beat, and ideally the journalist's name if known. This is the input that drives the live research — what this reporter has covered recently, what angles they've been building, what their readers care about. A release targeting a TechCrunch enterprise reporter needs a fundamentally different frame than one going to a trade publication covering the same industry vertical.
3
The broader trend this connects to
What's the larger story your news is a data point in? A solo founder's $2M raise doesn't interest TechCrunch as a standalone event. The same raise framed as evidence that vertical SaaS is finding funding while horizontal tools struggle is a different story — one that connects to something journalists and their readers are already tracking. The skill asks for this framing and builds it into the lead.
4
Quote direction and company voice
The skill asks what the spokesperson would actually say — not a PR-polished placeholder, but the real opinion or observation behind the announcement. It then shapes that raw input into a quote that gives the journalist something usable: an opinion with a point of view, a market observation with a specific claim, framed in language that sounds like a person rather than a press office.

Announcement vs Story — What the Difference Looks Like

Both examples below cover the same news: a B2B climate tech startup closing a $3.5M seed round. One is a press release written from inside the company. The other is written for a journalist covering climate infrastructure investment.

Without NovaKit — written from inside
FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE
CarbonTrack Raises $3.5M Seed Round to Accelerate Emissions Monitoring Platform

SAN FRANCISCO, May 27, 2026 — CarbonTrack, a leading provider of enterprise emissions monitoring solutions, today announced the closing of a $3.5M seed funding round led by GreenVenture Capital, with participation from several strategic investors.

"We're excited to close this round and continue our mission of helping enterprises achieve their net zero targets," said CEO James Holloway. "This funding will allow us to accelerate product development and expand our go-to-market efforts."

Result: announces the event. Tells the journalist nothing about why their readers would care or what larger story this connects to.
✓ With NovaKit Skill — written for the beat
FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE
As Enterprise Carbon Reporting Mandates Take Hold, CarbonTrack Closes $3.5M Seed to Own the Monitoring Layer

SAN FRANCISCO, May 27, 2026 — With CSRD enforcement now reaching mid-market companies and SEC climate disclosure rules advancing through review, enterprises are discovering that existing ERP systems can't produce the granular emissions data regulators require. CarbonTrack, which integrates directly with procurement and energy management systems to automate Scope 1, 2, and 3 tracking, has closed a $3.5M seed round led by GreenVenture Capital to scale ahead of the reporting wave.

"Most companies have set net zero targets. Almost none of them have the monitoring infrastructure to prove progress to regulators," said CEO James Holloway. "The mandate deadlines are creating urgency in a way that voluntary commitments never did — and that's fundamentally changed the sales conversation in the last eight months."

Result: opens with the regulatory context the beat has been tracking. The quote gives the journalist a specific, dateable claim. The journalist can file this as a market development piece, not just a funding announcement.

The right column contains the same facts. The funding amount, the company, the lead investor — none of it changes. What changes is the frame: the release opens with the market context that makes the funding meaningful, then presents the company as the specific solution to a specific and timely problem. The CEO quote offers a genuine market observation — "the mandate deadlines are creating urgency in a way that voluntary commitments never did" — that a journalist covering climate infrastructure can pull directly into a broader piece on regulatory-driven demand. That's a quote worth running. The left column's quote is a placeholder that a journalist will either cut or replace with their own paraphrase, which means they now have to do work the release should have done for them.

A complete, distribution-ready press release: a beat-calibrated headline, a lead paragraph that opens with the news hook rather than the company name, two to three supporting paragraphs that contextualise the announcement within the broader trend it connects to, a shaped quote from the named spokesperson, a second supporting quote if relevant, and a company boilerplate written in the factual, plain-language style journalists actually prefer. The release is formatted to wire service standards — dateline, structure, embargo note if required — and accompanied by a brief pitch note framing how to position the release in an email outreach to the target journalist.
