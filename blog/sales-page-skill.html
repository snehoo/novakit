The page has been live for two weeks. You've sent it to your list, posted it twice, run a small ad. Visitors are arriving — you can see them in the analytics. Average time on page is 47 seconds. Conversion rate is 1.1%. You know 47 seconds is enough time to read the hero section and leave. Something on that page is failing in the first three sentences, and you can't see what it is because you wrote it.

You go back to the copy. It looks right. Hero headline with the main benefit. Three bullet points. Social proof section. CTA button. Maybe the headline could be punchier. Maybe the CTA button colour. You make small edits, republish, wait. 1.3%. The problem isn't the details. It's the direction.

The copy was written from your product outward — what it does, what it includes, what makes it different from your perspective. A buyer landing on that page for the first time doesn't read it that way. They read it looking for one thing: evidence that whoever wrote this page understands exactly what they're struggling with right now. When they don't find that evidence in the first paragraph, they leave. Not because your product is wrong — because the page didn't speak their language before it tried to sell them something.

The Failure Mode No Design Change Fixes

Generic AI sales page copy has a recognisable structure that experienced buyers have learned to skim past: a bold claim in the hero, a "here's what you get" section that lists features as benefits, a proof block with testimonials that all sound the same, and a close that creates urgency without earning it. The structure itself signals "sales page" before a word is processed — and the moment a reader's pattern-matching fires, scepticism engages.

The specific failure is in the problem section, which generic copy either skips entirely or renders so broadly that it describes nobody in particular. "Struggling to grow your business?" describes every person on earth who runs one. It creates no recognition. A buyer who doesn't feel recognised in the first paragraph has no reason to believe the solution section applies to them either — so they read it with arms crossed, if they read it at all.

High-converting sales pages open in the buyer's own language, with the specific frustration that brought them here. Not "grow your business faster" — but the exact situation: the product that took three months to build and launched to silence, the consulting offer that gets interest on calls but loses people at the pricing page, the course that gets purchased and not completed. The reader who recognises their own situation in the first paragraph is already halfway sold before the product is mentioned.

💡
The core problem

A sales page written from your product outward describes what you built. A sales page written from your buyer's frustration inward describes what they've been living with. Only one of those makes the reader feel seen — and feeling seen is what earns the scroll to the offer.

This isn't a copywriting instinct most founders have naturally. It requires knowing how your specific buyers describe their own problem — the exact words, the specific context, the moment that made them go looking for something. That knowledge doesn't come from understanding your product well. It comes from researching the buyer before the page is written.

What Researching the Buyer First Changes on the Page

Before the Sales Page skill writes a headline or a bullet point, it researches two things: what your buyers are saying about the problem your product solves, and what the highest-converting pages in your category are doing differently right now. Those findings don't just inform the tone — they determine the structure of the entire page.

Buyer language research surfaces the specific phrases people use when they describe their frustration before they've found a solution. Not the polished language of someone who has solved the problem, but the raw version — the late-night forum post, the sales call objection, the exact sentence a customer used in their first email to you. When that language appears in your hero section, a reader landing on your page for the first time has the specific experience of feeling understood. That's not a soft outcome. It's the mechanism that keeps them reading past the fold.

Category research surfaces what the pages converting in your space right now are doing structurally — where the proof is placed, how objections are handled, which CTA framing is outperforming the obvious options, what social proof format carries more weight than a generic testimonial. These patterns shift as buyer behaviour changes, and a page built on conventions from two years ago is competing with pages built on what's working today.

The best-converting sales pages don't sound like sales pages. They sound like someone who already knows what you've been going through.

Together, those two research inputs produce a page that leads with the problem in the buyer's language, earns trust before making a claim, handles objections in the body rather than at the close, and uses proof that addresses the specific hesitation your buyer brings — not generic five-star praise.

3
Objection-First Architecture
The primary objection — price, trust, fit, or timing — is established in the calibrating questions and woven into the page body, not handled at the close. A reader who has their objection addressed before they've consciously formed it reads the rest of the page differently than one who reaches the CTA still holding it.

Quality Gate
Before output reaches you, the copy is checked against the most common conversion killers: hero sections that lead with the product instead of the problem, proof blocks that assert rather than demonstrate, CTAs that create urgency without establishing desire.
