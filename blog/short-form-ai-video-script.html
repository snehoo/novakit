The script came back in under ten seconds. Hook. Three points. Call to action. Neatly formatted, grammatically clean, utterly unusable. Not because the structure was wrong — it wasn't — but because it had no idea what platform you were posting on, what format was actually performing this week, or whether your audience responds to pattern-interrupt openers or story-first builds. It was a video script the same way a fish is a bicycle: technically something, functionally nothing.

Short-form video is the most format-sensitive content medium that exists right now. The same topic, the same message, the same creator — formatted for TikTok versus Reels versus YouTube Shorts — will perform completely differently because each platform's algorithm reads engagement signals differently, each audience has trained differently on what constitutes a watch-worthy first two seconds, and the native content conventions on each are distinct enough that crossposted scripts feel wrong to a platform's audience even when they can't articulate why.

Generic AI doesn't know any of this. It knows what a video script looks like. That's not the same thing.

Why Platform-Blind Scripts Underperform

The first two seconds of a short-form video are the entire game. Not because of some motivational content principle — because that's when the algorithm measures the completion signal that determines distribution. A video that loses viewers at second three gets suppressed. A video that holds them gets pushed. The hook isn't just a writing device; it's an algorithmic input, and what works as a hook on TikTok in May 2026 is meaningfully different from what worked in January, and different again from what works on Reels aimed at a different demographic slice.

This is the problem with scripts written from training data. The formats that AI learned from were already dated when the model was trained — and short-form format trends move faster than any model's training cycle. The "controversial statement" hook that saturated TikTok eighteen months ago now reads as a red flag for low-quality content to the algorithm and the audience simultaneously. The POV format that drove massive engagement on Reels two years ago is now a signal of lazy creative. Generic AI produces scripts that were current when the data was current. Which is to say: not now.

💡
The real problem

A script written without knowing this week's platform format norms isn't just mediocre — it actively signals low-quality content to the algorithm before a human viewer ever decides whether to watch.

Beyond the hook, there's pacing. TikTok audiences have trained on rapid cuts and pattern interrupts. YouTube Shorts audiences, pulled from a different browse context, have slightly longer patience for premise setup before the payoff. LinkedIn video — even short-form — performs better with a slower, more deliberate rhythm because the feed context is professional and the audience is not in scroll-entertainment mode. A single script written for "short-form video" without knowing the platform is optimised for none of them.

What Live Research Changes About the Output

Not what performed historically — what's in distribution right now. Current hook patterns, pacing conventions, caption structures, CTA placements, and the specific verbal and visual formats that the algorithm is currently rewarding in your category.

That research changes the output in ways that are immediately visible. A script for a financial educator on TikTok targeting 25-to-34-year-olds gets different hook language than a script for the same educator on YouTube Shorts targeting the same audience — because the browse context, the scroll speed, and the established native content conventions are different on each platform. The skill knows the difference and writes for it, rather than averaging across both.

Platform-native means the script sounds like it belongs there — not like it was written somewhere else and formatted to fit.

The three-question interview at the start of the skill establishes your platform, your niche, and your content goal — whether you're driving profile follows, link-in-bio clicks, saves, or comments, because each of those objectives changes how the CTA is written and where it lands in the script's pacing. A save-optimised script plants value early and delivers a recap CTA. A follow-optimised script builds personal connection before the ask. Those are structurally different scripts, not the same script with a different last line.

Same topic, same creator, same 45-second length target. One written by vanilla AI, one by the skill. The gap isn't in polish — it's in whether the script actually belongs on the platform it's destined for.

The left script uses a hook that was already a cliché in 2024 and gets lower initial completion rates because the "did you know" pattern has been conditioned out of the TikTok audience's attention — they've seen it thousands of times and their thumb moves before their brain does. The CTA on the right doesn't ask for a follow — it gives a reason to follow, which is structurally different and performs differently. The caption uses two hashtags because the current platform data shows that over-hashtagging in finance content depresses reach rather than expanding it.
