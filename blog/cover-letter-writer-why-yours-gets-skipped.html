Four Reasons Hiring Managers Skip Cover Letters in 20 Seconds

The decision to keep reading or move on happens fast — usually in the first paragraph, sometimes in the first sentence. Here are the four patterns that trigger the skip, and what the letters that get read do instead.

A hiring manager reviewing applications isn't reading cover letters the way the person who wrote them imagines. They're scanning. They have 60 to 80 applications to move through, a role to fill, and a limited window before other priorities take over. The cover letter gets 15 to 25 seconds — enough to read the opening paragraph and decide whether the rest is worth the time.

That decision is fast because the signal they're looking for is simple: did this person write this letter for this job, or did they write a letter and send it here? The skip happens when the answer is clearly the second. These are the four patterns that produce that answer in the first read.

The Four Patterns That Trigger the Skip

1
The opening sentence announces the application rather than making a point
"I am writing to express my interest in the [Role] position at [Company]." The hiring manager already knows this. They opened the file. An opening sentence that restates the fact of the application wastes the only moment where the letter has undivided attention — and signals immediately that what follows is a template rather than a considered communication. The skip decision is made before the second sentence.
What the read letters do instead
They open with the point. The first sentence states something specific — an observation about the company, a connection between the candidate's background and the role's actual challenge, a concrete claim about what the candidate brings — that requires the reader to engage rather than skip. The application context is implied, not stated.
2
The company name appears once and could be swapped for any other
Most cover letters mention the company once, in a sentence structured as: "I am excited about the opportunity to join [Company] and contribute to [vague mission statement paraphrased from the About page]." That sentence is the tell. It shows the candidate read the website — which takes four minutes — but didn't engage with what the company is actually doing right now, why this role exists at this moment, or what the hiring manager is actually trying to solve. The company name is present but the company is absent.
What the read letters do instead
They reference something specific and current — a product direction, a market move, a recent announcement, a challenge that's visible from the outside if you look. Something that demonstrates the candidate engaged with the company as it exists today, not as it describes itself in evergreen copy. That specificity is the primary signal that the letter was written for this application.
3
The skills section mirrors the job description
"Your posting mentions experience with X, Y, and Z — I have extensive experience with X, Y, and Z." This structure is meant to demonstrate alignment. It demonstrates keyword matching. A hiring manager who wrote the job description recognises their own language being reflected back at them, and the response is not "this candidate is a great fit" but "this candidate read the job description." Mirroring the requirements confirms that the candidate can read; it doesn't confirm that they can do the job, understand the role, or have thought about it beyond the bullet points.
What the read letters do instead
They identify the underlying problem the requirements are trying to solve — the actual reason the role exists — and connect specific experience to that problem, not to the surface-level skill list. "You need someone who can X" matters less than "here is the specific moment I solved the thing X is for, and here is what the outcome was."
4
The letter has no perspective on the role
A cover letter that describes the candidate's background accurately but never states a point of view about the role, the company, or the problem being solved reads as passively interested rather than genuinely engaged. The hiring manager is trying to assess judgment, not just qualifications. A candidate who has an opinion about the role — who can say something about what they expect the hardest part to be, what they'd focus on first, or why this particular version of this job at this company is interesting — demonstrates the kind of thinking the hiring manager wants to see in the person they hire. A letter that has no perspective on any of this gives them nothing to judge on that dimension.
What the read letters do instead
They include one sentence — sometimes two — that stakes out a position. Not a prediction ("I believe I will excel in this role") but a perspective ("The challenge I'd expect to spend the most time on in the first six months is X, which is where my specific background is most directly applicable"). That sentence tells the hiring manager how the candidate thinks, which is the information a cover letter can provide that a CV cannot.
💡
What all four share

Every skip trigger above is a symptom of the same root problem: the letter was written about the candidate rather than about the role. The letters that get read are written about the problem the role is trying to solve — and position the candidate as the specific person who can solve it.

Why Generic AI Makes All Four Worse

Ask a generic AI to write a cover letter and it produces something that hits all four skip patterns reliably. It opens by announcing the application. It mentions the company once in a sentence that could apply to any company. It mirrors the job description requirements back in slightly varied language. It has no perspective on the role because it has no information about the role beyond what's in the job posting — which is the same information the hiring manager already has.

Generic AI knows what cover letters look like. It doesn't know what this company is doing right now — which is the only information that makes a cover letter worth reading.

The fix for all four patterns requires the same thing: research done before writing. Knowing what the company is actually working on right now changes the opening. Knowing what the role is actually trying to accomplish changes the skills framing. Knowing the company's current context gives the candidate something to have a perspective on. Without that research, a cover letter defaults to the template — because the template is all that's available when the writer doesn't know the specific thing they're writing about.
