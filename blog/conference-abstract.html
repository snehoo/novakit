The Abstract That Describes Your Research — and the One That Gets Accepted

Programme committees aren't looking for research summaries. They're building a conversation programme, and they accept the abstracts that argue for a contribution to that specific conversation. A programme committee reviewing abstract submissions isn't asking "is this research good?" That question is for the full paper review. The abstract review asks a narrower question: does this work contribute something to the conversation this conference is trying to have? An abstract that accurately describes excellent research but fails to connect it to that conversation doesn't get accepted — not because the research is wrong, but because the abstract didn't make the case for its relevance.

This is the distinction most researchers miss when writing their first conference abstracts, and that many researchers keep missing long after. The abstract is not a summary of the paper. It's an argument for why the paper matters to this audience, at this conference, in this year's programme. Those are different documents, and writing one when you need the other is why solid research gets rejected at submission stage.

Generic AI makes the problem worse in a specific way. It's very good at summarising. Ask it to write a conference abstract and it produces an accurate, well-structured summary of the research you describe to it. That summary will clear the quality bar on every dimension except the one that actually determines acceptance: it won't be positioned within the ongoing debates and live questions the conference is organised around, because generic AI doesn't know what those are.

What Programme Committees Actually Evaluate

Conference acceptance decisions are made by researchers who have been immersed in their field's current debates for years. They read abstracts fast — 200 to 300 words in under two minutes — and they're pattern-matching against a mental model of what the field needs right now. An abstract that opens by positioning the work within a live theoretical debate, identifying a genuine gap in current approaches, or addressing a methodological limitation that the field has been wrestling with signals immediately that the author is in the conversation. An abstract that opens with a description of the research topic, however accurate, signals that the author may be doing good work in isolation from the conversation.

The committee is building a programme, not a catalogue. They're selecting presentations that will speak to each other — that advance a shared inquiry rather than presenting parallel monologues. An abstract that demonstrates awareness of where the field currently stands, and argues that this research moves it somewhere it needs to go, is doing the committee's curation work for them. That's the abstract that gets accepted.

💡
The core problem

An abstract written as a research summary is accurate about the paper and silent about the field. An abstract written as a contribution argument positions the paper within the live conversation the conference exists to advance. Programme committees are selecting for the second — and most AI-assisted abstracts produce the first.

The challenge for any researcher — and for any AI writing tool — is that making this argument requires knowing what the current conversation actually is. Which debates are live in this subfield right now. Which methodological questions are unresolved. Which theoretical frameworks are being challenged, extended, or replaced. That knowledge is dynamic, discipline-specific, and conference-specific. It's not in the paper. It has to come from somewhere else.

What Field Research Changes About an Abstract

Before the NovaKit Conference Abstract skill writes a sentence, it researches the conference's current thematic focus and the field's live debates: recent publications in the relevant journals, the conference's own call for papers and recent programme themes, the methodological and theoretical tensions that are currently generating the most scholarly discussion, and how the work being submitted sits relative to that landscape.

That research changes two things in the abstract that determine acceptance. The opening — which needs to establish the live gap or debate the work addresses, not just introduce the topic. And the significance statement — which needs to explain what the work contributes to the ongoing conversation, not just what it finds. Both require knowing what's currently happening in the field, which is what live research provides and training data from 18 months ago cannot.

The accepted abstract doesn't summarise research. It argues for a contribution to a conversation the committee is already invested in — and that argument requires knowing what the conversation currently is.

The skill also calibrates to discipline convention. Abstract norms differ meaningfully between the sciences, social sciences, and humanities — in structure, in how findings are reported, in whether a clear conclusion is expected or whether the abstract is permitted to frame an open question. A conference abstract for an empirical psychology paper is structured differently from one for a literary studies paper, which is structured differently from one for an engineering conference. The skill applies the right conventions before writing, not after.

Summary Abstract vs Contribution Abstract

Same research — a mixed-methods study on remote work's effect on junior employee mentorship in technology companies — submitted to an organisational behaviour conference. The difference between a summary abstract and a contribution abstract is visible from the first sentence.

Without NovaKit
This paper examines the impact of remote work on mentorship experiences among junior employees in technology companies. Using a mixed-methods approach combining survey data from 312 employees across 14 companies with in-depth interviews, we investigate how distributed work environments affect the frequency, quality, and perceived value of mentorship relationships.

Our findings suggest that remote work reduces informal mentorship opportunities while formal mentorship programmes show mixed results. We identify three distinct adaptation patterns among junior employees and discuss implications for organisational practice. These findings contribute to our understanding of remote work's effects on employee development and organisational socialisation.
✓ With NovaKit Skill
The rapid normalisation of hybrid and remote work has surfaced a structural tension that prior research on distributed teams largely ignored: mentorship, particularly the informal developmental contact that accelerates early-career growth, depends on spatial proximity in ways that formal programmes cannot replicate. This paper reports findings from a mixed-methods study — 312 junior employees across 14 technology firms, supplemented by 28 semi-structured interviews — that maps exactly where the proximity-dependence breaks down and where it does not.

We identify three adaptation patterns that predict whether junior employees experience remote arrangements as developmental stagnation or accelerated autonomy, and show that the mediating variable is not programme structure but manager discretion in creating structured informal contact. For a conference programme examining post-pandemic organisational redesign, this finding reframes the intervention point from policy to management practice.

The summary abstract describes the research accurately and gives a programme committee nothing to evaluate beyond topic relevance. The contribution abstract opens by naming the structural tension current literature has missed, establishes the study's empirical basis, identifies the specific finding that challenges the dominant policy framing, and closes by explicitly connecting the contribution to the conference's programme theme. A reviewer reading it knows immediately what the paper argues, why it matters to the current debate, and why it belongs in this programme.
