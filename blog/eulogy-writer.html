You have three days. Maybe fewer. Someone you loved has died, and at some point in the next 72 hours you're going to be asked to stand in front of people who loved them too — and speak. The weight of that is enormous. The blank page is worse.

So you turn to AI. You type something like "write a eulogy for my father, he was a kind man who loved fishing and his family." And what comes back is technically a eulogy. It has all the right parts. An opening line about gathering to celebrate a life. A paragraph about his warmth. A line about how he's gone but not forgotten. It's not wrong. It's just not him.

The problem isn't the writing. The problem is that the AI had nothing to write from. It produced a tribute to a concept — a warm father who liked fishing — not to the actual person who had a specific laugh, a phrase he repeated, a way of showing up that was entirely his own. When that gets read at a service, the people in those chairs will nod politely and feel nothing specific. That's the failure a eulogy can't afford.

Why Generic Falls Apart at the Worst Moment

A eulogy has exactly one job: make the room feel the specific weight of this person's absence. Not the abstract sadness of death, but the particular loss of this voice, this habit, this presence that will not come back. That job cannot be done with generic output.

Generic AI output for emotional writing fails the same way every time. It reaches for the most statistically likely phrasing — the sentences that appear most often in condolence speeches, memorial pages, and tribute templates across the internet. It produces "a life well-lived" and "those who knew him best" and "his memory lives on in all of us." These phrases aren't wrong. They're just drained of meaning from overuse. When someone in the third row hears them, they feel the distance between the words and the person they're grieving.

💡
The core problem

AI generates the most probable tribute. A eulogy needs the most true one — and those are rarely the same thing.

What makes a eulogy land isn't eloquence. It's specificity. It's the detail that only someone who knew this person could include — the thing they always said, the way they took their coffee, the story that makes everyone laugh before they cry. Those details cannot come from a model that knows nothing about the person. They have to be drawn out first.

What the Three-Question Interview Changes

The NovaKit Eulogy Writer skill doesn't start writing when you load it. It starts asking. Before it produces a single sentence of the tribute, it runs a calibrating interview — three questions designed to extract the specific context that separates a real eulogy from a generic one.

Those questions change what gets written at a fundamental level. The skill isn't working from "kind man, loved fishing." It's working from: what did he say that you still hear in your head? What would people who knew him recognise immediately? What's the thing about him that's hard to explain to someone who never met him? The answers to those questions give the skill something to work from that no vanilla AI session ever gets.

The skill that asks the right questions produces output that couldn't exist without the answers — and that's exactly what a eulogy needs to be.

This matters because the people in that room already know the person you're describing. They don't need a description. They need recognition — the feeling that whoever wrote these words actually knew him too. The interview is how that recognition gets built into the output before writing starts.

The Difference a Specific Detail Makes

Here's what generic looks like alongside what this skill produces, using the same starting point — a tribute for someone's father:

Without NovaKit
My father was a man of great warmth and quiet strength. He loved his family deeply and showed it through his actions every day. Whether it was teaching us to fish or simply being present, he was always there when we needed him. His memory will live on in each of us, and the lessons he taught us will guide us for the rest of our lives. He was taken too soon, but he leaves behind a legacy of love that will never fade.
✓ With NovaKit Skill
My dad had a saying for everything. If you complained about the cold on one of his early-morning fishing trips, he'd say "the fish don't care what time it is — neither should you." I didn't understand that until I was about thirty. That was how he taught — sideways, through doing, in sentences you'd carry around for years before they made sense. He never gave speeches. He just showed up. Every time, without being asked, without expecting credit. That's the thing I keep coming back to.

The left side describes a good father. The right side describes this father — the one with the fishing saying, the indirect teaching style, the specific quality of showing up. Anyone in that service who knew him would hear the second one and feel it land. The first could have been written about anyone.

That gap — between a description and a recognition — is what the interview creates. The skill didn't invent that fishing saying or the specific lesson about patience. Those came from the questions. What the skill did was know how to ask for them, and then know what to do with the answer.

What You Actually Walk Away With
