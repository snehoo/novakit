You've written the brief. Target keyword at the top, a few bullet points about what to cover, maybe a note on tone. You hand it to Claude — or a freelancer — and wait. What comes back is a 1,500-word article that covers the topic correctly, reads cleanly, and will rank for nothing. It answers the question your keyword implies without understanding how Google is already answering it, what the top-ranking pages are doing structurally, or which angle is conspicuously missing from every result on page one. The content is fine. It is completely invisible.

This is the brief problem, not the writing problem. The article failed upstream — at the point where someone decided what to build — and no amount of editing fixes a strategy that was never validated against the SERP. Most briefs are topic containers dressed up as instructions. They say what the article is about; they say nothing about why a particular structure, at a particular depth, from a particular angle, is the one that actually has a chance of ranking in the position you want.

The SEO Blog Post Brief skill doesn't fix the writing. It fixes what comes before the writing, which is where the ranking outcome is actually decided.

What a Brief Without Research Actually Produces

Ask Claude to write an SEO brief for almost any keyword without first examining the SERP, and you'll get a document that looks comprehensive. There will be a suggested H1, a list of subtopics to cover, maybe a recommended word count. It will be organised, logical, and based entirely on what a reasonable person would assume the article should contain — not on what Google is demonstrably rewarding for that keyword right now.

The gap between those two things is where content budgets go to die. A "best CRM for small business" brief written from assumptions will propose a standard product roundup, probably suggest seven to ten tools, and recommend a word count that mirrors the topic's apparent breadth. What the SERP actually shows at position one might be a tightly scoped article covering four tools with a depth-per-tool that generic briefs never specify, anchored by a comparison table structured around the three attributes small business buyers actually use to decide. The winning article isn't longer or shorter than your assumed brief would produce — it's architecturally different, because it was built from evidence.

💡
The core problem

A brief built from assumptions tells the writer what you think should rank. A brief built from SERP research tells the writer what Google has already decided is the right answer — and exactly where the gap is that your article can own.

The consequence is predictable. Content produced from assumption-based briefs enters a market it doesn't understand, competes on general quality rather than strategic differentiation, and either stalls outside the top twenty or earns short-lived traffic before better-targeted competitors displace it. The writing was never the issue. The brief was the issue.

What Live SERP Research Changes About the Brief

Before the SEO Blog Post Brief skill writes a single heading suggestion, it examines what is actually ranking for your target keyword — the H2 and H3 structure of the top results, which subtopics every ranking page covers (the floor your article must meet), which subtopics appear in only one or two results (the differentiation opportunity), and what format Google is rewarding: list-based, comparison tables, definitional, how-to. This is not inference from training data. It is live research conducted every time you run the skill, against the SERP as it stands today.

That research changes the brief in four concrete ways. The angle shifts from "cover this topic" to "cover this specific gap that no ranking page has addressed well." The heading structure reflects what the SERP consensus requires rather than what a topic outline would suggest. The depth-per-section is calibrated to what's actually competing, not to a generic word count. And the differentiation note identifies the one thing your article can do that page-one results don't — because without that, you're building a slightly better version of something that's already losing to the pages already there.

The brief is the strategy document. The article is just the execution. Getting the brief right is worth more than any amount of writing quality applied to the wrong structure.

What this means in practice is a brief that a writer — or Claude writing from it — can execute without additional research or judgment calls. The heading structure is ready. The angle is defined and differentiated. The depth target per section is explicit, tied to what the SERP shows rather than a round number. The writer's job becomes execution, not strategy — which is both faster and more reliable than asking a writer to do both at once.

Assumption Brief vs Research Brief

The difference between a brief built from assumptions and one built from SERP research isn't subtle. Here's the same keyword — "email marketing for e-commerce" — handled both ways.

Without NovaKit
Target keyword: email marketing for e-commerce

Cover: Why email marketing matters for e-commerce. Types of emails (welcome, abandoned cart, post-purchase). Tips for writing subject lines. How to segment your list. Tools to use. Best practices.

Tone: Helpful, practical
Length: ~1,500 words
✓ With NovaKit Skill
Angle: The three automation sequences that account for most e-commerce email revenue — and which one most brands skip entirely (post-purchase re-engagement).

H2 structure: Why most e-commerce email programmes underperform (consensus gap: specificity) → The welcome sequence: what high-converting versions actually include → Abandoned cart: timing and copy decisions the data supports → Post-purchase re-engagement: the sequence page-one results don't cover adequately → Segmentation that drives revenue vs. segmentation that just adds complexity

Differentiation: Every top-3 result treats post-purchase email as a single send. Cover it as a 3-part sequence with specific timing and copy direction per message.

Internal links: Link from existing /email-tools and /ecommerce-growth pages.

The assumption brief is a topic container. A writer following it will produce something coherent that competes with everything already on page one without understanding what any of it is doing. The research brief is a strategic document: it knows where the gap is (post-purchase sequences treated as a single email by every competitor), specifies the exact structural difference to execute on, and tells the writer precisely what the article needs to do that the current top result doesn't. Those are different articles, with different ranking prospects, produced from the same keyword.
