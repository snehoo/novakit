You've spent weeks on Pinterest. You know roughly what you want — open up the kitchen, replace the flooring, maybe add a bathroom downstairs. You sit down with Claude, describe the project, and get back three beautifully written paragraphs about your vision. Then you hand it to a contractor and they look at you blankly. That's not a brief. That's a mood board in prose.

Contractors need to know which trades are involved, in what order, what materials they're specifying against, and what the structural or permit implications might be. Without that, they can't give you an accurate quote. They guess. You get a number that either blows your budget or comes in suspiciously low — and you find out why three weeks into the project.

The Home Renovation Brief skill was built to produce the document a contractor actually needs, not the one that feels satisfying to write. It researches before it writes: current material specifications, typical trade sequencing for the scope you've described, permit considerations by project type, and the dependency chain that determines what has to happen before what.

Why Vague Briefs Cost Real Money

The most common renovation mistake isn't choosing the wrong tile. It's entering the contractor conversation without a document that defines scope. When scope isn't defined, it shifts. And every time scope shifts after work starts, you're paying for it — either directly in variation orders or indirectly in delays while trades wait on decisions that should have been made before anyone picked up a tool.

A kitchen renovation that involves removing a wall requires a structural engineer's report before a builder can quote accurately. A bathroom addition on a ground floor might require new drainage runs depending on the existing fall. These aren't obscure edge cases — they're the things contractors discover on day one when the brief they received said "add a downstairs bathroom." The variation order lands on your desk. You had no idea it was coming.

The gap between what homeowners write and what contractors need to quote is not a communication problem — it's a document problem. A well-structured brief eliminates the ambiguity that turns renovations into budget nightmares.

Generic AI output doesn't know any of this. It produces aspirational descriptions of finished spaces, not the scaffolding a contractor uses to plan, quote, and sequence work. The Home Renovation Brief skill knows the difference — because it researches the project type before it writes the document.

What Live Research Changes for Renovation Planning

When you describe your renovation to the skill — scope, rough budget, property type, what stays and what goes — it doesn't start writing. It starts researching. Current material specifications for what you're describing. The trade sequence for that scope: does tiling come before or after the plumber's second fix? Which elements require a structural assessment before a builder can price? What permits are typically required for works of this type?

That research shapes the structure of the document it produces. A loft conversion brief looks different from a kitchen remodel brief. A brief for a period property flags different structural considerations than a new-build extension. Generic prompting produces one template for everything. This skill calibrates the document to the actual project.

A contractor who receives a scoped brief with material specs and a trade sequence can quote in an afternoon. A contractor who receives a vision statement is just guessing — and you're paying for the guess.

The practical result: the brief you hand over contains enough information for multiple contractors to quote against the same document. Apples-to-apples comparison. You can see where quotes differ and ask specific questions, rather than trying to reconcile wildly different assumptions about scope.

What the Home Renovation Brief Skill Produces

Three calibrating questions before anything is written: what's the scope, what's the property context, and what constraints matter (budget ceiling, timeline, whether the space needs to remain partially usable during works). Then the skill builds the document.

1
Scope definition
A clear written description of what the project includes — and importantly, what it excludes. Scope creep starts when what's excluded isn't written down. This section defines the boundary of the project so both parties are working from the same understanding.
2
Material specifications
Not "timber flooring" but the specification level contractors need: engineered or solid, finish type, subfloor requirements, expansion gap allowances. Not "new kitchen units" but the supply-and-fit or supply-only split, finish standard, and whether appliances are client-supplied. These aren't guesses — they're derived from live research on what your project type typically requires.
3
Trade sequencing
The order in which trades need to work, with dependencies flagged. First fix electrics before plastering. Plumbing rough-in before tiling. Structural work signed off before the builder can proceed. This section gives your contractor the information to plan the programme rather than discover conflicts mid-project.
4
Budget framework
A line-item breakdown by trade and material category, with contingency guidance built in. Not a quote — a framework that helps you evaluate the quotes you receive and identify where a contractor might be light on contingency or missing a line item entirely.
5
Permit and structural flags
Likely permit requirements for the project type, and any structural considerations that need professional sign-off before work can start. Flagged as action items, not buried in prose — so nothing gets missed in the contractor conversation.

Project Scope Rear single-storey extension (approx. 4m × 5m) with removal of existing load-bearing wall between kitchen and dining room. Scope includes: groundworks and foundations, structural steel to support removed wall, extension structure, roof, glazed rear elevation (bifold system), first and second fix plumbing and electrics, UFH to extension slab, kitchen supply-and-fit (client-specified units), island with integrated appliances.
