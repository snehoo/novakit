You paste a LinkedIn post into Claude and ask it to write three more in the same style. The output comes back polished, coherent, and completely unrecognisable as yours. The sentence rhythm is off. The vocabulary is too formal, or too casual. There's an enthusiasm you never use. You spend twenty minutes editing it back into something you'd actually publish — at which point you've spent more time than if you'd written it from scratch.

This isn't a Claude problem. It's a context problem. Claude doesn't know that you never use exclamation points. It doesn't know your audience is mid-level ops managers who distrust hype. It doesn't know you always lead with a specific scenario rather than a general claim. Every session, it starts from scratch — a blank slate with no memory of your voice, your audience, or the dozen invisible rules that make your writing yours.

The Brand Voice Guide skill builds the document that fixes that. One file. Every session, before Claude writes a word for your brand, it reads that file first.

What Happens When Claude Has No Voice Context

Claude's default register is helpful, balanced, and professionally neutral. That's the right default for a general-purpose tool. It's the wrong setting for a founder who has spent three years building a distinctive point of view, or a freelance strategist whose tone is half the reason clients hire them.

Without voice context, Claude averages. It writes at the median of whatever it has been trained on for your topic — which means your output sounds like a competent professional who could work anywhere, for anyone, in any industry. That's not a brand. That's wallpaper.

💡
The core problem

Claude isn't writing in the wrong voice because it can't learn yours — it's writing in the wrong voice because no one told it what yours is. That's an input problem, not a model problem.

The fix isn't better prompts in the moment. A one-line instruction like "write in a conversational tone" gives Claude almost nothing — conversational to a fintech founder and conversational to a yoga instructor are completely different registers. The fix is a structured document that answers the questions Claude actually needs answered before it can write in your voice with any precision.

What Live Research Changes About a Voice Document

Most brand voice guides get built once, filed, and forgotten. The Brand Voice Guide skill doesn't produce a static document you paste into prompts and hope for the best. Before it builds your guide, it researches what's actually working for voices like yours right now — the sentence structures that are landing, the register shifts happening in your category, the framing patterns your audience is responding to versus tuning out.

That research is what separates a voice document that was accurate in 2023 from one that's calibrated to how your audience reads today. Tone isn't timeless. What reads as authoritative in one moment reads as stiff twelve months later. What reads as casual now would have seemed unprofessional three years ago. The skill researches before it builds — which means the document it produces reflects current signal, not stale convention.

A voice document built on current research doesn't just describe how you write — it describes how your audience is reading right now.

The result is a guide Claude can actually use. Not a list of adjectives ("authentic, bold, conversational") that could describe anyone, but a specific, operational document with examples, anti-examples, and calibration notes that give Claude something to work from rather than guess at.

What the Brand Voice Guide Skill Actually Builds

The skill asks three calibrating questions before generating — about your audience, your existing content, and the specific outputs you use Claude to produce. Then it builds a structured voice document with five components, each one designed to answer a specific question Claude will ask every time it writes for your brand.

1
Tone Descriptor Stack
Not adjectives — specific behavioural descriptors. Not "direct" but "leads with the conclusion, never the context." Not "warm" but "acknowledges the reader's situation before offering a solution." The difference is that Claude can act on one of these and can only guess at the other.
2
Banned Phrase List
The words and phrases your brand never uses — and crucially, what you use instead. "We never say 'solutions' — we say what the thing actually does." Claude can follow a substitution rule. It can't follow "avoid corporate jargon" because it doesn't know which jargon you consider corporate.
3
Audience Profile
Who you're writing for, what they already know, and what they're sceptical of. Claude adjusts vocabulary, assumed expertise, and framing based on this — the difference between writing for a first-time founder and writing for a seasoned operator is substantial, and the skill builds the profile that captures it.
4
Structural Patterns
How your writing is typically organised — whether you open with a question or a claim, how long your paragraphs run, whether you use headers or prefer a flowing essay structure. These are the invisible patterns that make a piece of writing feel like yours even before someone reads the words.
5
Rewrite Examples
Three before/after rewrites showing generic Claude output transformed into your voice. These examples do more work than any descriptor — they show Claude the pattern in action, so it can replicate the transformation rather than just read about it.

Generic Output vs Voice-Calibrated Output

The difference is easier to see than to describe. Here's the same brief — a short LinkedIn post about raising prices — handled two ways.

Without Brand Voice Guide
Exciting news — we've updated our pricing to reflect the value we deliver! As our services have grown and improved, it was time to align our rates with the quality our clients have come to expect. We're grateful for your continued support and can't wait to keep delivering exceptional results. 🙌
✓ With Brand Voice Guide
We raised our prices last month. No announcement, no countdown, no apology. The work got better, the results got more consistent, and the old rate stopped making sense. If you've been thinking about working together, the time to reach out is before the next adjustment, not after.

The first is what Claude produces when it has no voice context and defaults to enthusiastic, inoffensive, professional-neutral. The second is what it produces when it knows the brand leads with facts rather than sentiment, never uses exclamation points, and treats the audience as people who respect directness over warmth. Same brief. Completely different result — because the skill gave Claude what it needed to write with precision rather than guess.

Who Gets the Most from Brand Voice Guide

✓
Built for

Founders and solopreneurs who use Claude regularly but spend significant time editing output back into their voice. Freelancers and consultants whose tone is a competitive differentiator — clients hire them partly because of how they communicate. Content leads managing a brand that has a defined voice but no document Claude can actually use.

The clearest indicator that this skill is worth your time: you already know what your brand voice is, but you've never written it down in a way a language model can act on. You know you'd never say "solutions" or "leverage." You know your paragraphs run short. You know you open with a claim, not a question. But Claude doesn't know any of that — and rebuilding that context in every session, post by post, costs you the time the AI was supposed to save.

This skill is less useful if your brand voice is genuinely still forming, or if you're producing one-off content where voice consistency doesn't matter. It's most useful when you're producing a steady volume of content — LinkedIn posts, newsletters, client communications, proposals — where the cumulative cost of sounding generic is real.

The Output You Walk Away With

The skill produces one structured document: your brand voice guide. It's formatted specifically to be pasted at the top of any Claude session before you give it a writing task. No summarising, no re-explaining, no hoping Claude remembers what you told it last time. You paste the guide, then the brief. Claude reads both, and it writes from your voice rather than the average of everything it's been trained on.

The document runs to roughly 600–800 words — long enough to be specific, short enough that Claude processes it cleanly without the kind of context dilution that happens with very long instructions. It includes the five components above, plus a one-paragraph "how to use this document" note so you can hand it to a collaborator or VA without a separate explanation.

The version of Claude that's been editing your content into something usable is the version of Claude that doesn't have this document. Once it does, the editing stops being the work — the writing is.
