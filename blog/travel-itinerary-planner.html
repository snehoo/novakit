You've landed in Lisbon. You open the AI-generated itinerary you spent 45 minutes prompting last week. Day 1 starts with the Museu Nacional do Azulejo — closed Mondays, which today is. The first café on the list closed permanently in early 2025. And the "quick walk" between attractions turns out to be a 35-minute uphill climb that your 68-year-old travelling companion is not going to enjoy.

This is what happens when AI plans from memory instead of from the current state of a place. The facts aren't wrong, exactly — the museum does exist, the walk is technically possible — they're just not useful for the trip you're actually on.

The Travel Itinerary Planner skill works differently. Before it produces a single hour-by-hour block, it researches your specific destination — current seasonal conditions, what's open, what's worth the trip right now versus what shows up in every round-up because it ranked well in 2021. The result isn't a template with your city's name dropped in. It's a plan built for where you're going, when you're going, and who's going with you.

What Vanilla AI Gets Wrong About Travel Planning

Ask any AI assistant to plan a 4-day trip to Tokyo and you'll get the same itinerary that thousands of other people are looking at right now. Tsukiji Market at 7am. Senso-ji before the crowds. Shibuya crossing at dusk. Ramen somewhere in Shinjuku.

None of that is bad advice. It's just not advice — it's a greatest-hits compilation compiled from training data that doesn't know your pace, your interests, whether you're travelling with kids, or that the sakura finished blooming two weeks ago so the timing everyone told you to plan around isn't actually relevant this trip.

💡
The core problem

AI travel plans are optimised for looking comprehensive, not for being executable — and there's a significant difference between the two.

The real failure shows up in the logistics. An itinerary that puts the Uffizi Gallery and the Accademia on the same morning in Florence sounds efficient. In practice, both require advance booking, both are 45-minute waits even with bookings, and the walk between them is through the city's most congested tourist corridor. A plan that looks reasonable on paper produces a terrible first day.

What Live Research Actually Changes

The Travel Itinerary Planner skill runs live research before it writes. That means it's working from current information about your destination — not from what was true about it when the model was trained.

For travel planning, that distinction matters more than in almost any other domain. Restaurants open and close. Seasonal conditions shift the entire character of a place. New exhibitions open. Popular sites become overcrowded to the point of being pointless. The "hidden gem" everyone was writing about in 2023 now has a two-hour queue and a merchandise stand outside.

A plan that knows your destination is in its rainy season, that the top museum requires tickets booked three days out, and that your hotel neighbourhood is genuinely walkable to the places you care about — that's a different product than a list of famous things near a city name.

What that live context unlocks is an itinerary that's actually paced for a real trip: the right number of things per day for the pace you've specified, in a sequence that makes geographic sense, with the logistics flagged before they become problems on the ground.

How the Travel Itinerary Planner Skill Works

The skill opens with three calibrating questions before it touches your itinerary. Your answers are what determine whether it builds a plan for two slow-paced retirees exploring a city's food scene or a family with two young kids who need afternoon nap windows baked in. Same destination, fundamentally different outputs.

1
Live destination research
Before generating a single hour, the skill researches your specific destination — current season, what's accessible, where the known friction points are, which experiences are genuinely worth the time versus which are coasting on old reputation.
2
Trip profile calibration
Pace preference, group composition, interest weighting — cultural immersion vs. food-first vs. outdoor focus — all pulled from your three-question interview. The skill can't produce a calibrated plan without knowing who's going and what actually matters to them.
3
Day-by-day construction
Each day is built around geographic logic — minimising unnecessary transit time — with realistic time allocations per attraction, not optimistic ones. Morning energy is used for higher-effort experiences; afternoons account for heat, fatigue, and the reality that most travellers slow down after lunch.
4
Logistics flagging
Advance booking requirements, seasonal closures, neighbourhoods that need specific transit knowledge, known tourist traps worth skipping — these surface in the plan rather than waiting to surprise you on arrival.

What You Actually Walk Away With
