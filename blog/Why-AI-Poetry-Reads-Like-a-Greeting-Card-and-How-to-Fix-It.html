You asked Claude to write a poem for your colleague's retirement. You got eight lines about "journeys" and "chapters" that end with something rhyming "day" and "way." It's not offensive. It's not memorable. It reads like it was written for anyone who has ever retired, which is to say it was written for no one in particular.

That's the specific failure of vanilla AI verse: it reaches for universality and lands on blandness. The rhymes arrive on schedule. The sentiment is broadly appropriate. And the person reading it can tell, immediately and viscerally, that no one thought about them when they wrote it.

The fix isn't asking Claude to "try harder" or "be more creative." It's giving the skill the context it needs before it writes a single word — and that's precisely what the Poetry & Verse Prompt skill does first.

What Generic Verse Actually Sounds Like

When you ask Claude to write a poem without any calibration, it draws on the statistical centre of poetry — the forms, rhythms, and images that appear most often across the training data. That means iambic-adjacent metre, ABAB or ABCB rhyme schemes, and metaphors built around light, paths, seasons, and time. The result is technically verse. It's just not verse for this.

The problem compounds with occasion. A wedding poem, a birthday poem, a poem for a child's first day of school — Claude treats these as variations on a single template rather than fundamentally different emotional tasks. A wedding poem needs to carry weight, because it's read aloud in front of everyone the couple loves. A child's first-day poem needs to be light and brave and honest about the morning being hard. These aren't the same poem. But without calibration, they often come out that way.

💡
The core problem

Generic AI poetry fails not because Claude lacks vocabulary or rhythm — it fails because it has no information about the specific person, occasion, or emotional register that would make the poem land.

The person who asked for help wanted something that felt personal. They got something that felt produced. That gap is what the skill closes.

Why Context Changes Everything in Verse

Poetry is a compression format. Every line has to carry more weight than prose because there are fewer of them. That means every choice — the form, the tone, the images, whether it rhymes or doesn't — needs to be right for this recipient, this occasion, this emotional note. A sonnet about grief lands differently than a free-verse piece. Both can be right. Neither is automatically correct.

The Poetry & Verse Prompt skill researches current form conventions and what kinds of verse are resonating for different occasions before it starts drafting. It isn't pulling from static training patterns. It's working with live signals about what's actually moving people — which wedding poems are being shared, which structures feel fresh versus tired, which imagery has been overused to the point of meaninglessness.

The difference between a poem that makes someone cry and one they politely thank you for is almost never the vocabulary — it's whether the form and register were chosen for them.

Then comes the three-question interview. Before a single line is drafted, the skill asks what it needs to know: who this is for, what the occasion is, and what emotional note it should carry. Is this funny or tender? Formal or intimate? Should it rhyme, or would rhyme make it feel forced for this particular person? The answers to those questions change the output more than any single writing instruction could.
