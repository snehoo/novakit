The Sales Page That Sounds Like It Was Written About Your Product

Generic AI sales copy describes your offer accurately and converts poorly. The gap isn't the writing quality — it's the direction. Pages written from the product outward tell buyers what you built. Pages written from the buyer's frustration inward make them feel seen.

You read back the draft. It's clean. The headline names the benefit. The bullet points list what's included. The testimonial section has three quotes. The CTA button says "Get started today." You know, reading it, that it sounds like a sales page — and you know that's not quite right, but you can't name what's missing.

What's missing is the moment before the headline. The specific situation a buyer is in when they arrive on your page — the thing that's been going wrong, the cost of that thing going wrong, the exact words they would use to describe it to someone who asked. That's not in the draft because nobody researched it. The AI wrote the page about the product. A buyer lands on it looking for evidence that someone understands the problem.

When they don't find that evidence in the first two sentences, they make a decision so fast they don't notice making it. The page becomes one of the thirty other tabs they'll close without buying from. Not because your product is wrong — because the page didn't speak to them before it tried to sell them something.

The Problem Section Is Where Every Generic Page Fails

Ask any AI to write a sales page and it will produce a recognisable structure: a benefit headline, a problem section, a features list dressed as benefits, social proof, a CTA. That structure isn't wrong. The problem section is.

Generic problem sections describe frustration from the outside. They read like a narrator explaining a character's pain rather than the character living inside it. "Are you struggling to convert your traffic into customers?" is accurate — and it describes nobody specifically enough to create recognition. A buyer reading that sentence knows it was written for anyone who sells anything. It does not make them feel seen. It makes them feel categorised.

Compare that to a problem section that opens: "You built the thing. You launched it. You got the 'this is exactly what I needed' replies. Then the follow-up silence." That's written from inside the experience. A founder who has lived that sequence reads the second sentence and feels something shift — this page knows what the past three weeks have felt like. That shift is the mechanism that earns the scroll. Without it, a buyer reads the offer section with crossed arms. With it, they read it already half-convinced.

💡
The core problem

AI writes sales pages about products because products are what you brief it with. Buyers don't arrive on your page looking for a product description — they arrive carrying a specific frustration. The page that names that frustration before anything else is the one that keeps them reading.

This isn't a copywriting instinct most founders develop naturally, because it requires deliberately stepping out of product knowledge — the thing founders have most of — and into buyer language, which requires research that happens before the page is written.

What Changes When Buyer Research Comes First

When the Sales Page skill runs, it surfaces buyer language before it touches page structure. That means pulling how people in your buyer's situation describe the problem in their own words — not the polished version they'd write in a case study, but the raw version: the forum post, the sales call objection, the message a prospect sends the week before they decide not to buy.

That language becomes the problem section. Not a paraphrase of it — the actual words, the actual situation. A SaaS founder whose offer is pipeline coaching doesn't open with "struggling to close deals?" The buyers who need pipeline coaching are opening their CRM every morning and seeing the same three opportunities that have been sitting at the proposal stage for forty days. That's the problem section. That specific sentence is why they're on the page at all.

Category research runs alongside it — what the highest-converting pages in your offer type are doing structurally right now. Where proof lands harder, how the offer is scoped and sequenced, which CTA framing earns the click versus which creates resistance. Page architecture follows from both inputs: buyer language determines the opening, category patterns determine the order.

The first sentence of your problem section is a test. Buyers pass or fail you on it before they've read your headline.

What that research unlocks isn't better writing — it's a page that passes the test. A buyer who recognises their own situation in the first paragraph reads every subsequent section differently. Scepticism is a posture that forms after you feel like you're being sold to. Recognition disarms it before it forms.

The Problem Section That Fails vs The One That Earns the Scroll

A freelance strategist is launching a productised offer: a four-week go-to-market sprint for early-stage SaaS founders. Same offer, two ways into the problem section.

Written about the product
Are you struggling to find your first customers?

Getting early traction is one of the hardest challenges for SaaS founders. Without a clear go-to-market strategy, even the best products fail to reach the people who need them.

That's where I come in. With 10+ years of experience helping early-stage startups find product-market fit, I offer a proven, step-by-step approach to building your GTM strategy — tailored to your specific market and stage.

Let's build your growth engine together.
✓ Written from inside the frustration
You've had the calls. The ones where someone says "this is exactly what I've been looking for" and you think: finally.

Then nothing. The follow-up goes unanswered. Or they reply once and go quiet. Or they say they need to "think about it" and you never hear from them again.

Your product didn't fail those calls. Your path from interested to paying did — and that path is a go-to-market problem, not a product problem.

That's the only thing we work on in four weeks. Not positioning theory. Not a deck. The specific sequence of moves that turns "this is exactly what I've been looking for" into a payment confirmation.

The first version is written for a category — "SaaS founders with GTM problems." The second is written for a specific person in a specific moment: someone who has experienced the "this is exactly what I need" call followed by silence, and who has started to wonder whether the product is the problem. That doubt — the product, not the path — is the exact objection the page needs to address. It's addressed in the third paragraph, before a service is mentioned, before any credentials are listed. A founder reading that page who has lived that sequence doesn't encounter the offer with scepticism. They encounter it with relief.

It's the wrong fit if the offer itself isn't defined yet — if what's included, who it's for, and what outcome it delivers are still being worked out.
