Why Do AI-Generated Wedding Vows Sound Generic?

AI vows sound generic because they're written without any information about your partner. When you ask a standard AI to write vows, it has no context — no story, no moment, no specific quality, no shared history. So it writes about love in general: warmth, kindness, making you a better person, choosing you every day. These things are true of most relationships. They're true of yours. They're also true of everyone else's, which is why the output could have been written for any couple at any wedding.

Specificity is what separates vows from a greeting card. The moment your partner knows was only the two of you. The thing they do that no one else in the room knows about. The decision they made that told you something important before you had words for it. None of that is available to an AI that starts writing immediately — and no amount of editing a generic draft will add it, because adding specific details to a generic structure still produces a generic structure with details bolted on. The specificity has to be architectural, not cosmetic.

💡
The actual problem

Generic AI vows aren't bad writing. They're writing about the wrong subject — love in general rather than this person specifically. No prompt tweak fixes that. The fix is giving the AI real information about your partner before it writes a single sentence.

What Makes Wedding Vows Actually Personal?

Personal vows contain at least one detail that your partner will recognise as unmistakably theirs. Not a general quality — a specific instance of that quality. Not "you make me feel safe" — the particular thing they did, or said, or decided, that created that feeling. The distinction matters because generalities are forgettable and specifics are not. An audience hears "you make me feel safe" and thinks, yes, nice. An audience hears a specific story about a specific night and goes quiet in a different way entirely.

Three elements reliably produce personal vows. First, a grounding moment — a real memory that captures something essential about your partner or your relationship, placed early so everything that follows is anchored in something true. Second, a named quality — not "kindness" but the specific form their kindness takes, the version of it you see that other people in the room might not. Third, a promise rooted in history — a commitment that means something because of what you've already been through together, not a promise that could apply to anyone starting out.

The vow that makes the room go quiet isn't the one about forever — it's the one that names a specific moment your partner thought only the two of you remembered.

Vow Tone Styles — Which One Fits Your Ceremony?

Tone is the second thing generic AI gets wrong. Vows that don't sound like your natural register are almost as uncomfortable as vows that aren't specific — your partner knows how you speak, and so does everyone who knows you both. The Wedding Vows Writer skill calibrates to your tone before drafting. Here's what each style produces and who it fits best.

Tone style What it sounds like Best for
Sincere and earnest Direct, emotionally open, no deflection — every sentence means exactly what it says Couples who want the ceremony to feel weightful and unhurried; traditional or religious settings
Warm with moments of humour Grounded in love but willing to name something real or funny — a specific moment that gets a small laugh before it lands emotionally Couples who are naturally funny together; informal venues; guests who know them well
Simple and direct Short sentences, no ornamentation — says the true thing without building to it; quietly powerful People who find long emotional speeches uncomfortable; intimate ceremonies; partners who value understatement
Poetic and considered More literary cadence, careful word choice — feels written rather than spoken Writers, readers, or people whose relationship has a strong literary or artistic dimension

Most people find they want something between two of these — warm and direct, or sincere with one moment of levity. The skill handles combinations; you don't have to pick exactly one.
