You've applied to forty roles. You've heard back from three. You've convinced yourself the market is terrible, the industry is oversaturated, or that you're somehow not quite qualified enough for jobs you know you could do. The CV has been updated — expanded, refined, sent through a free AI writer, proofread twice. It still isn't working.

The most common reason a qualified candidate doesn't get to interview isn't the recruiter's judgment. It's that the recruiter never sees the application. Applicant Tracking Systems — the software sitting between your CV and the hiring manager's inbox — filter out the majority of submissions before a human makes any decision at all. A well-credentialled CV written as a general document, rather than a response to a specific job description, routinely scores below the threshold for human review. Not because the candidate is wrong for the role. Because the document wasn't written to say so in the language the system is scoring against.

Generic AI doesn't fix this. It produces a cleaner version of the same problem — a well-formatted document that describes your career accurately and matches no particular job description precisely.

What ATS Systems Are Actually Scoring

Applicant Tracking Systems don't read CVs the way a hiring manager does. They parse the document for specific signals: the presence or absence of keywords lifted directly from the job description, the way those keywords are distributed across sections, the formatting conventions that allow reliable text extraction, and in more sophisticated systems, the degree of match between claimed skills and the seniority of the role being advertised.

The keywords that matter most are not the obvious ones. A job description for a "Senior Product Manager" doesn't just want to see the phrase "product management." It wants to see the specific tools named in the requirements section ("Jira," "Figma," "OKRs"), the exact methodology language used by that company ("agile ceremonies," "sprint retrospectives," not just "agile"), and the outcome framing their industry uses for seniority signals ("P&L ownership," "cross-functional alignment," "roadmap prioritisation"). Generic CVs use general language. The job description uses specific language. ATS systems score the gap between them.

💡
The core problem

A CV written about your career history is not the same document as a CV written in response to a specific role. Only the second one scores well in ATS — and writing it properly requires analysing the job description before the first sentence is drafted.

This is not a flaw in the system that can be gamed. It's the mechanism the system is designed around. The fix isn't keyword stuffing — sophisticated ATS platforms and the recruiters who review flagged applications both recognise that immediately. The fix is understanding what the role actually requires at this company, in this industry, at this seniority level right now, and writing achievement-led bullets that demonstrate those requirements using the vocabulary the employer has already signalled they value.

Why Role Research Changes Everything

The NovaKit Resume & CV Builder skill doesn't start with your work history. It starts with the job. Before a bullet point is drafted, the skill analyses the specific job description you're targeting — extracting the required skills, the preferred tools, the seniority signals, the outcome language, and any industry-specific vocabulary that needs to appear in the final document. That analysis shapes every section that follows.

A CV that earns an interview isn't more impressive than one that doesn't. It's more precisely matched — and the matching has to happen before the writing starts.

It also calibrates to seniority. A CV for a graduate role and a CV for a Director-level position are different documents structurally, not just in content. Graduate CVs lead with education and potential; senior CVs lead with commercial impact and scope. The skill adjusts the weighting of each section, the depth of achievement evidence required, and the summary framing based on where in the career ladder the target role sits — because a Director-level CV written with graduate-level structure signals to a recruiter that the candidate doesn't understand the seniority they're applying for.

The research also covers what the current market expects for this type of role. CV conventions shift. The one-page rule that was gospel for certain industries has softened in others. The objective statement that used to open every CV is now actively penalised at senior levels in many sectors. The skill reads current norms before writing — not the conventions from a career advice article written three years ago.

Marketing Manager — Acme Co (2021–2024)

• Responsible for managing marketing campaigns across multiple channels including social media, email, and paid advertising.

• Worked collaboratively with cross-functional teams to develop and execute brand strategies that improved brand awareness and customer engagement.
✓ With NovaKit Skill — job description analysed
Marketing Manager — Acme Co (2021–2024)

• Led integrated campaigns across paid social, email, and OOH for three product lines — growing aided brand awareness 18 percentage points (YouGov tracker, 2023) against a flat category.

• Owned brand P&L for a £2.4M campaign budget; reallocated 22% mid-cycle from declining display to performance social, recovering Q3 ROI to 3.1x against a 2.6x target.

The generic version is accurate and describes real work. It would be invisible in an ATS scan for a Senior Brand Manager role at a consumer goods company — none of the language maps to the job description's requirements. The tailored version uses the exact vocabulary the role demanded: "brand P&L," "aided brand awareness," "YouGov tracker," "campaign budget ownership." These aren't embellishments. They're the same work, described in the language the hiring company used when they wrote the job description. The ATS scores the match. The recruiter reads the proof.
