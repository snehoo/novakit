Why Generic AI Renovation Planning Fails at the Contractor Meeting

The document you write and the document a contractor needs to quote are two completely different things. Generic AI produces one of them. Here's where it breaks down — and what the other one actually looks like.

A contractor walks into your kitchen for the first time. You've prepared. You spent an evening with Claude writing up your renovation plans — the open-plan layout, the bifold doors, the new kitchen island, the downstairs bathroom. It reads beautifully. You hand it over. The contractor reads it in about forty-five seconds and says, "Right. So what are the structural drawings showing for that wall removal?"

There are no structural drawings. You didn't know you needed structural drawings at this stage. And now the meeting that was supposed to end with a quote has turned into a lesson in what a contractor actually needs before they can price anything. You'll go back, get the engineer involved, and try again in three weeks — if the contractor is still available.

This isn't bad luck. It's the gap between a renovation description — which is what generic AI produces — and a renovation brief, which is what contractors use to plan and price work. That gap has a cost, and it's usually measured in delays, variation orders, and the uncomfortable discovery that two contractors quoted very different things because they made very different assumptions about scope.

Four Places Generic AI Output Breaks Down

Generic AI is genuinely useful for many writing tasks. For renovation planning, it runs into four specific failures — not because it writes badly, but because it doesn't know what a contractor actually needs to read.

Failure 1
It describes the finished result, not the scope of work
Generic output tells a contractor what the space should look like when finished. That's not a brief — that's an aspiration. A brief tells them what work is in scope, what is explicitly out of scope, what the existing conditions are, and what the structural or service implications of those changes are. Without scope definition, the contractor defines scope themselves — and their definition and yours may not match until a variation order arrives.
Failure 2
Material references are aesthetic, not technical
"Timber flooring throughout" could mean solid hardwood at £120/m², engineered oak at £55/m², or LVT at £30/m². The gap between those numbers across a 60m² ground floor is over £5,000 in materials alone, before fitting. Generic AI names materials by appearance, not specification. Contractors quote against specifications. Every vague material reference in your brief is an assumption the contractor makes — and quotes against — without you knowing.
Failure 3
Trade sequencing is absent entirely
A kitchen extension involving plumbing, electrics, underfloor heating, tiling, and joinery has a specific order these trades must follow. First fix before plastering. Screed before tiling. Steel installation signed off before the builder removes the wall. Generic AI doesn't know this sequence and doesn't produce it. Without it, your contractor has to build the programme from scratch — and if you're managing multiple trades yourself, you have no document to coordinate them against.
Failure 4
Permit and structural flags are missing
Removing a load-bearing wall requires a structural engineer's calculation and sign-off. A rear extension beyond permitted development limits requires planning permission. A bathroom addition may trigger building regulations. These aren't optional extras — they're legal prerequisites that determine whether work can start. Generic AI doesn't surface them because it isn't reasoning about your specific project type, property situation, or local requirements. You discover them when a contractor asks, or worse, when building control shows up.
💡
The core problem

Generic AI writes for a reader who wants to understand the vision. A brief is written for a reader who needs to price, plan, and execute the work. These are not the same document — and the confusion between them is where renovation projects go wrong before they start.

What the Contractor Is Actually Reading For

When a contractor reviews a brief, they're answering four questions in sequence: What am I being asked to do? What are the structural or service constraints I need to account for? Which trades are involved and in what order? And can I price this accurately against a defined scope, or am I guessing?

A generic AI output typically answers none of these cleanly. The scope is implied rather than defined. The structural constraints aren't surfaced. The trade sequence doesn't exist. And because the material specifications are aesthetic rather than technical, the contractor is essentially creating their own brief from your vision statement and pricing against their interpretation.

When three contractors quote the same project and the numbers are £30,000 apart, the usual explanation isn't incompetence — it's that they were quoting three different briefs they each invented from the same vague document.

A well-structured brief fixes this. Not because it removes all ambiguity — experienced contractors will always have clarifying questions — but because it eliminates the structural ambiguity that produces wildly different quotes and the surprise variation orders that appear three weeks in.

What a Contractor-Ready Brief Contains

The difference between a renovation description and a renovation brief is structural, not stylistic. Here's what each section needs to contain for a contractor to actually use it.

Document element Generic AI output Contractor-ready brief
Scope definition Describes desired end result ("open plan kitchen/diner") Lists works in scope line by line; explicitly names exclusions
Material specification Aesthetic reference ("timber flooring," "bifold doors") Technical spec: product type, grade, finish, subfloor requirement, supply-and-fit or supply-only split
Trade sequencing Not present Named trade order with dependencies flagged (e.g. first fix M&E before plastering, screed before tiling)
Structural flags Not present Load-bearing elements identified; SE sign-off requirements noted as action items
Permit requirements Not present Likely planning and building regulations requirements identified by project type
Budget framework Single budget figure Line-item breakdown by trade and material category with contingency guidance

Scope of Works
Rear single-storey extension (~4m × 4.5m GIA). Removal of load-bearing wall between kitchen and sitting room. Ground floor WC addition (internal, near utility). New kitchen supply-and-fit. UFH to extension slab only.

Excluded: Existing sitting room decoration, garden landscaping, appliances (client supply), external lighting.

Structural pre-requisites: SE calculation required for load-bearing wall removal and steel specification. Party wall notice required (shared boundary, east elevation).

Permit flags: Rear extension likely exceeds PD limits — planning application required. Building regs required for structural works, new drainage run (WC), and glazing spec.

Trade sequence: Groundworks → structure + roof → weathertight → first fix M&E + UFH pipework → insulation + screed (28-day cure) → wall removal + steel → second fix → kitchen fit → WC fit-out → decoration → snagging.

Material specs (for quoting): Bifold system — thermally broken aluminium, triple glazed, client-specified RAL. UFH — water-fed, manifold to existing boiler, thermostat per zone. Kitchen — supply-and-fit, client-specified units, solid surface worktop.

The generic version gives a contractor a conversation starter. The skill version gives them a document they can quote against. The structural pre-requisites section alone — flagging the need for a structural engineer's calculation and a party wall notice — represents work that must happen before a builder can mobilise. Knowing that upfront means you initiate those processes now rather than discovering them as a delay after work was supposed to start.
