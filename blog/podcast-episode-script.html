You spend two hours preparing for a guest interview. You read their LinkedIn, skim a couple of old podcast appearances, and draft a rough list of questions in a notes app. Then you ask Claude to turn it into a proper script. It comes back with an intro that could belong to any show, opener questions so broad the guest could answer them in their sleep, and a closing segment that sounds like it was lifted from a journalism 101 handbook. You rewrite most of it yourself. The AI saved you nothing.

The problem isn't Claude. It's that Claude had nothing to work with. No sense of your show's voice. No knowledge of what this specific guest has said before — and, crucially, what they haven't. No awareness of the format your audience actually responds to, or what's been covered to death on every other show in your niche. You handed it a blank canvas and expected a portrait.

A podcast script isn't a writing problem. It's a research problem with a writing problem attached. The Podcast Episode Script skill solves both — by doing the research first.

What Generic Scripting Actually Produces

Ask vanilla Claude for a podcast script and it will give you structure. You'll get an intro block, a guest bio read, a handful of questions grouped loosely by theme, and a sign-off. It looks like a podcast script. If you squint at it, it even feels like one. Then you try to record with it and realise the questions are all things the guest has been asked forty times. The opener bio is a recitation of their LinkedIn summary. The "insightful" follow-up questions are the exact softballs every host defaults to when they haven't done the work.

That's not Claude being bad at writing. That's what happens when a script is built without context. The guest's real story — the pivot they haven't talked about, the opinion that runs counter to received wisdom in their industry, the specific tension between their background and their current work — none of that is in the output. Because none of it was in the input.

💡
The core problem

A podcast script written without live research into the guest and current format trends asks the same questions in the same order as every other episode your listener already skipped.

Listeners are not patient. They know within the first three minutes whether this episode is going to give them something they haven't heard. A generic opener — "So tell us how you got started" — is a signal that you didn't prepare, and they'll act accordingly.

Why Live Research Changes the Script

Before the Podcast Episode Script skill writes a single question, it researches. It looks at what your guest has said publicly — what positions they've taken, what they repeat in every interview, and where the gaps are. It maps current format conventions for your show type, because what works in a founder interview format is structurally different from what works in a solo narrative show or a debate-style episode. These aren't interchangeable.

Static scripting — giving Claude a name and a topic and asking it to write — produces output anchored to training data. The format trends it knows are the ones that were circulating when it was trained. Guest research is whatever happens to be in its parametric memory. For a well-known figure, that might be something. For most guests on most podcasts, it's close to nothing.

The question that makes a guest lean forward is never the obvious one — it's the one that only exists if you've done the research.

Live research means the skill knows what your guest said in their last three public appearances, which means it can write the question that goes somewhere new. It knows what format patterns are currently driving engagement in your category, which means the pacing and structure of the script reflects what's actually working — not what worked two years ago.

What the Podcast Episode Script Skill Does

The skill asks three calibrating questions before it generates anything. Your show format, your guest's name and context, and the angle you want this episode to take. Those three answers transform the output from a generic interview template into something that fits your specific show with your specific guest on this specific topic.

1
Guest research pass
The skill researches the guest's recent public statements, published work, and prior interview appearances — specifically to find what's been covered and what hasn't. This is where the non-obvious questions come from.
2
Format and tone calibration
Interview show, solo narrative, co-hosted conversation, debate panel — each has a different rhythm. The skill adapts its structure to your format, not to a one-size template. The intro block for a founder interview reads differently from a panel discussion opener.
3
Question architecture
Questions are sequenced intentionally: warmup that establishes rapport, tension-building mid-section questions that go somewhere the guest isn't expecting, and a closing arc that lands on something the audience will remember. Not ten random questions in a list.
4
Anti-slop quality gate
Before the script reaches you, it's checked for opener clichés, lazy bio reads, and generic follow-ups. The version you see is the version that passed. You don't wade through a draft that needs to be rebuilt.

The Output You Walk Away With

The skill delivers a complete episode script: a fully written intro block (not a fill-in-the-blanks template, but prose you can read or adapt), a guest bio that highlights the specific background relevant to this episode's angle rather than reciting their full career, a structured question sequence of eight to twelve questions with suggested follow-up hooks embedded, and a closing segment with a clear sign-off that connects back to the episode's central theme.

Most creators end up using sixty to seventy percent of it verbatim and improvising around the edges — which is exactly how a good prep document should work. You know where the episode is going. The guest doesn't feel like they're being processed.
