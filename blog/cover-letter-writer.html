The Cover Letter Written for Any Job — and the One That Couldn't Be

A hiring manager reads the opening paragraph and knows immediately whether the letter was written for this role or adapted from a template. The NovaKit Cover Letter Writer researches the company and job before writing a single sentence.

A hiring manager reviewing 80 applications on a Tuesday afternoon has a fast and accurate heuristic: does this letter mention anything that could only apply to this job, at this company, right now? If not, it was written for a category of job rather than this one. It goes in the no pile — not because the candidate is unqualified, but because the letter signals they didn't think the role was worth specific attention.

The cover letter that gets read is the one that couldn't have been sent elsewhere unchanged. It references something specific about the company — a product direction, a recent hire, a market position — and connects it directly to why this candidate, with this background, is interested in this role at this moment. That specificity is not about flattery. It's about demonstrating that you did the work to understand what you're walking into.

Generic AI makes this harder, not easier. Ask Claude or ChatGPT to write a cover letter and you get something grammatically impeccable, structurally sound, and completely interchangeable with every other cover letter written for the same job title. It was trained on cover letters. It knows the format. It doesn't know the company, doesn't know what's happening in the industry right now, and defaults to the middle of the distribution — which is exactly where letters go to be ignored.

Why Generic AI Produces Generic Letters

The cover letter problem for AI is a research problem. A strong cover letter requires knowing three things that change from application to application: what the company is actually trying to accomplish right now, what the role is specifically responsible for delivering, and which aspect of the candidate's background maps most directly to that need. Generic AI knows none of these things. It knows what cover letters look like. It produces the shape of a cover letter without the content that makes one worth reading.

The output is recognisable to anyone who's reviewed applications in the last two years. The enthusiasm is stated rather than demonstrated. The skills match the job description word for word — which reads as keyword stuffing rather than genuine alignment. The company is mentioned by name exactly once, in a sentence that could have been written about any company in the sector. The closing paragraph expresses eagerness to discuss the role further. Nothing in the letter could only have been written by this person for this job.

💡
The core problem

A cover letter that reads as generic tells the hiring manager two things simultaneously: that the candidate used AI to write it, and that they didn't consider the role specific enough to do the research. Both signals work against the application.

The fix isn't better writing. It's research before writing. A letter that opens with a specific observation about the company's current direction — one that connects to why the candidate's background is relevant right now — can only be written by someone who looked. That's the signal the letter needs to send, and it requires knowing things about the company and role that aren't in the job description.

What Live Research Changes About a Cover Letter

Before the NovaKit Cover Letter Writer drafts a single sentence, it researches the company and role: recent news, product direction, hiring patterns, the team's stated priorities, and the specific language the company uses to describe its own work. It reads the job description not just for requirements but for what the role is actually trying to accomplish and what kind of person the hiring manager is imagining as they write it.

That research changes the opening paragraph — the only paragraph most hiring managers read before deciding whether to continue. Instead of "I am excited to apply for the [Role] position at [Company]," a research-backed letter opens with something that demonstrates prior engagement: a specific observation about the company's direction and a direct statement of why this candidate's particular background is relevant to it. The hiring manager reads it and thinks "this person looked us up" — which is the first thing a cover letter needs to communicate.

The cover letter that gets read is the one that couldn't have been sent anywhere else unchanged — and that specificity requires research, not better writing.

The skill also calibrates to career stage. A graduate cover letter making a first professional transition reads differently from a senior professional pivoting industries, which reads differently from someone returning after a career gap. The same job application process involves fundamentally different communication challenges at different career points, and the letter needs to address the right one.

What the Cover Letter Writer Skill Produces

The skill's interview covers four inputs: the job posting or role description, the candidate's relevant background and key experience, the company name and any context about why this role specifically, and career stage. From those:

1
Live company and role research
Pulls current signal on the company — recent announcements, product focus, team growth patterns, how they talk about their work publicly — and uses it to ground the letter in something specific and verifiable. Not boilerplate enthusiasm. Actual context.
2
Role-to-background matching
Maps the candidate's specific experience to the role's actual priorities — not a keyword match against the job description, but an identification of which part of the candidate's background is most relevant to what the role is actually trying to accomplish. The letter leads with that match.
3
Career stage calibration
Adjusts tone, evidence selection, and framing to the candidate's actual stage. A graduate letter needs to translate academic or internship experience into professional relevance. A senior professional's letter needs to convey judgment and track record, not enthusiasm. A career-changer's letter needs to address the transition directly rather than hoping the reader doesn't notice.
4
Anti-slop quality gate
Runs the draft through a check for the patterns that mark AI-generated cover letters: stated rather than demonstrated enthusiasm, mirror-matching of job description language, a closing paragraph that adds nothing, and any sentence that could appear in a letter for a different role unchanged. These are rewritten before output.
5
Two complete letter variants
A direct variant — concise, confident, leads immediately with the strongest match — and a narrative variant that builds a short story around the career moment that makes this application make sense. You pick the one that fits the company culture, or edit elements from both.
