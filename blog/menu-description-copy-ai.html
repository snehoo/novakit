A restaurant in east London rewrote its entire menu in an afternoon. Not the dishes — just the descriptions. The lamb shoulder that had sat at twelve percent order rate for six months became the second most-ordered main within two weeks. Same dish, same price, same position on the menu. Different twelve words underneath the name. The chef had been describing what was in the dish. The rewrite described what it was like to eat it.

Menu copy is the only piece of hospitality writing that has a direct, measurable effect on revenue per cover. Every other piece of brand writing — the website, the social posts, the email newsletter — operates at a distance from the transaction. The menu is read at the moment of decision, by someone who is already seated, already committed to spending money, actively looking for reasons to order. It's the highest-conversion sales surface a restaurant has, and most of them treat it as an administrative document.

Generic AI makes this worse, not better. Ask vanilla Claude to write a menu description and you get a list of components, possibly with an adjective or two attached to the most prominent ones. "Pan-seared duck breast with cherry reduction, dauphinoise potatoes, and seasonal greens." That sentence contains accurate information and zero appetite appeal. It answers the question "what's in this dish?" rather than the question the diner is actually asking: "is this the one I want?"

What Generic Menu Copy Gets Wrong

The failure of generic AI menu copy isn't factual inaccuracy — it's the wrong job entirely. Menu descriptions do two things, in this order: they create desire for the dish, and they justify the choice. A description that lists components does the second job while skipping the first. It gives the diner information about what they're buying but never makes them want to buy it.

Desire in a menu description is created through sensory language, flavour hierarchy, and texture contrast — the specific combination of words that makes a reader's mouth respond before they've consciously decided anything. "Slow-braised" signals tenderness and depth. "Crisp-edged" signals texture contrast. "Bright" used about a sauce signals acidity and freshness against something rich. These are precise tools, not decorative ones. Generic AI produces adjectives the way a spell-checker produces suggestions: statistically plausible, contextually inert.

💡
The real job of menu copy

A menu description's first job is not to inform — it's to create appetite. The information is necessary; the appetite is what makes someone choose this dish over the one above it on the page.

Venue tone compounds the problem. The description that works for a relaxed neighbourhood bistro is wrong for a tasting menu restaurant, and both are wrong for a wedding reception caterer's evening buffet card. The casual bistro wants warmth and directness — "sticky, slow-cooked short rib, chips, béarnaise" has earned its place on that menu. The tasting menu wants precision and restraint — every word carries weight because there are very few of them. The wedding caterer needs approachable elegance: descriptions that read well on a printed card held by someone in formal wear who doesn't want to feel like they're decoding a Michelin guide. Generic AI produces one register and applies it everywhere.

What Cuisine and Tone Calibration Changes

The Menu Description Copy skill takes three inputs before writing anything: the dish and its key components, the cuisine style and cooking approach, and the venue or occasion tone. Those three inputs determine vocabulary register, description length, flavour emphasis, and whether the copy leads with technique, provenance, texture, or occasion.

A Japanese-influenced dish at a high-end restaurant gets different treatment from a Jamaican jerk dish at a street food market, even if the structural components of the descriptions are similar. The former calls for restraint and precision — the description does less work because it trusts the cuisine's reputation to carry some of the appetite signal. The latter calls for heat, energy, and directness — the description needs to communicate the experience of eating it, not just the method of preparation. Calibrating to cuisine style isn't aesthetics. It's matching the description to the register the dish and its diner already exist in.

The best menu descriptions don't describe the dish. They describe the experience of eating it — and let the diner imagine themselves there.

Occasion tone changes the length and formality more than anything else. A printed wedding menu card reads in a quiet moment — guests have time, the setting rewards considered language, and the description is part of the event's atmosphere rather than a sales tool competing for attention. A pub gastropub menu reads in a noisy room while someone is also talking to you — it needs to land in three seconds or it hasn't landed at all. Both contexts require good copy. They require completely different kinds of good copy. The skill knows the difference because the three-question interview establishes it before a word is written.

How the Menu Description Copy Skill Works

Three inputs, then a research pass for current tone conventions in your cuisine category, then up to a full menu section of descriptions — with a length variant for each dish so you have options for different menu formats.

1
Dish, components, and cooking method
The name of the dish, its main and supporting components, and how the key element is cooked. The cooking method is the appetite signal — "wood-fired," "slow-braised," "cold-smoked," "charred" each trigger a distinct sensory anticipation before the rest of the description does anything. Getting this right requires knowing it, not inferring it.
2
Cuisine style and venue character
Whether the cuisine is European, Asian, Middle Eastern, modern British, or any other tradition; whether the venue is a fine dining restaurant, a casual bistro, a wedding reception, a street food stall, or a corporate catering menu. These two inputs set vocabulary register, description length target, and the flavour hierarchy the description leads with.
3
Live research on current copy conventions
Before writing, the skill identifies the current tone and language patterns being used in top-performing menus in your cuisine category and venue tier. Menu copy conventions evolve — the farm-to-table provenance emphasis that dominated a few years ago has given way to technique-led descriptions in many fine dining contexts. The skill writes for where things are now, not where they were.
4
Two length variants per dish, allergen-aware
Each description comes in a full version and a condensed version — because a website menu, a printed card, and a chalkboard specials board have different length tolerances for the same dish. Allergen flagging is built in: key allergens are noted in the description format that matches your venue's convention rather than appended as an afterthought.

The left description is accurate and generic — it could have been written for any lamb dish at any restaurant. The right description does several things the left doesn't: "gives way at the touch of a spoon" creates a specific tactile anticipation that "slow-cooked until tender" doesn't.
