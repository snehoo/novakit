What a Holiday Greeting Written in 2022 Looks Like in 2026

The signals of warmth have shifted. What read as personal four seasons ago now reads as template — and the gap between the two is not one a static approach can close.

Here is a professional Diwali greeting from 2022, written with genuine care by someone who wanted it to land well:

"Dear Priya, wishing you and your loved ones a joyous Diwali filled with light, warmth, and prosperity. May the festival bring new beginnings and continued success to everything you do. It's been a pleasure working together this year — looking forward to all that lies ahead. Warm regards."

Nothing wrong with it. In 2022, it was good. It hit the right beats — the occasion acknowledged, the personal relationship noted, the forward-looking close. Someone who received it would have felt, if not moved, at least genuinely seen. Four seasons later, that same message reads as assembled. Not because the writer stopped caring. Because everyone else caught up.

How the Register of Professional Warmth Shifts

Writing conventions for greetings don't change by dramatic rupture. They erode. Someone writes something that feels slightly fresher than the standard format — shorter, less formally warm, more willing to name a specific moment — and a few hundred people receive it and remember it. Other writers notice. The approach gets imitated. Within two or three seasons it becomes the new standard, which means it becomes the new invisible.

The specific signals that dated first in professional greeting copy were the ones that felt most considered in 2021 and 2022. The explicit naming of the festival's meaning ("the festival of lights," "a time of renewal") started reading as encyclopaedic rather than personal. The stock forward-looking close ("looking forward to all that lies ahead") collapsed under its own weight after being used by approximately every business that sent holiday mail that year. The warm-regards sign-off, which carried genuine professional warmth when it was less ubiquitous, became wallpaper.

💡
Why this matters

A greeting that uses the signals of warmth from three seasons ago doesn't read as warm. It reads as someone who stored a template and is running it again. The recipient may not be able to name the problem, but they feel it — the message doesn't slow them down.

What's replaced those conventions is harder to describe but easier to recognise: a willingness to be more abrupt, to leave more unsaid, to reference something specific and then stop rather than expanding it into a full paragraph. The current register of a greeting that lands well tends to be shorter than the 2022 version. More willing to end on a single concrete detail. Less concerned with covering all the expected ground. It reads like it was written in five minutes by someone who knew exactly what they wanted to say — which is a different thing from reading like it was written quickly.

What a Static Approach Can't Track

This is the practical problem with asking vanilla Claude to write a holiday greeting, or reusing a structure that worked two years ago: neither has any access to how the register has moved. Claude's training has a cutoff. A saved template has a timestamp. Neither of them knows that the phrase "may this season bring renewed energy to your endeavours" now reads like a LinkedIn automation, even though it felt genuinely warm when someone first wrote it.

The shift isn't dramatic enough to be obvious in a single message. It accumulates across a recipient's inbox over a few seasons. They've received enough messages in that format — enough "may the festival bring prosperity to you and your family," enough "it's been a pleasure and I look forward to what we'll build together" — that the format itself has become a signal. Not a signal of warmth. A signal that the sender had something to say and reached for the nearest available structure rather than writing it.

The Signals That Dated — and What Replaced Them

To make this concrete: here's what the shift actually looks like across the elements of a professional greeting. This is the difference between a message that reads as current and one that reads as stored.

Element 2022 register (now dated) Current register (what lands now)
Opening "Wishing you and your loved ones a joyous [festival]..." First name only, or a direct situational reference — no preamble
Festival reference Explains the festival's meaning ("a time of light and renewal") Names the occasion and moves immediately to the personal — the reader knows what Diwali is
Year in review "It's been a pleasure working together this year" Names one specific thing that happened — a project, a moment, a shift — or skips it entirely
Forward close "Looking forward to all that lies ahead" / "to continued success" A single concrete sentence, or just the sign-off — the forward-look is implied, not stated
Length Three to four sentences, fully developed Two to three sentences, deliberately spare — says what it means and stops
Sign-off "Warm regards" / "With warmest wishes" First name only, or a brief informal close — "warm regards" has become its own dated signal

None of these shifts are enormous. Together they determine whether a message reads as written this year or filed away from a previous one. A greeting that hits three or four of the dated column lands with a friction its sender can't identify — a vague sense that it didn't quite work despite being correctly structured and genuinely meant.
