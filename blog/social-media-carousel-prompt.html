You spent 40 minutes writing carousel copy. Good hook, clear slides, a decent CTA at the end. You posted it and watched it get 60 impressions and four profile visits — from people who definitely didn't save it. Two days later you see an account with a fraction of your following post something that looks structurally similar, and it's pulling thousands of saves. The writing isn't better than yours. The ideas aren't more original. But something about the architecture is different — and you can't quite name what.

That gap isn't a writing quality problem. It's a format intelligence problem. Carousel slides that earn saves are built to match exactly how people navigate swipeable content right now — the entry hook format that's converting on a given platform this week, the slide cadence that keeps thumbs moving instead of dropping off, the final-slide structure that makes "save for later" feel like the obvious action. None of that is in Claude's training data from 18 months ago, and none of it transfers from one platform to another.

The Social Media Carousel Prompt skill doesn't start by writing. It starts by researching — pulling what's performing right now on the target platform before building the slide architecture your content will live inside.

Why Generic AI Carousel Output Gets Ignored

Ask vanilla Claude to write you a LinkedIn carousel about time management and you'll get something functional. A hook slide, four or five insight slides with clean structure, a closing slide that says something like "Which of these resonated most? Let me know in the comments." It reads fine. It would have worked in 2022. The problem is that LinkedIn's content patterns have shifted considerably since then — the hook formats that once stopped the scroll now feel familiar enough to scroll past, and the "engagement bait" CTA on slide 10 actively suppresses reach on certain algorithms.

The same dynamic plays out on Instagram, where the carousel format that earned massive saves 18 months ago (the numbered list with a bold text overlay on each slide) now competes with far more sophisticated visual-narrative structures. A slide deck built on yesterday's pattern doesn't just underperform — it signals to both the algorithm and the viewer that the content is recycled.

💡
The core problem

Generic AI writes carousel content as if slide format is static. But the formats that drive saves and shares on any given platform are shifting constantly — and a slide deck built on stale patterns reads as stale, regardless of the quality of the ideas inside it.

This is why editing the output rarely fixes it. You can tighten the copy, sharpen the hook, add a better CTA — and still be holding a slide deck built on a structural framework that no longer matches how people actually engage with carousels on your platform. The architecture is the problem, not the words.

What Live Research Changes About Slide Architecture

Before the Social Media Carousel Prompt skill writes a single slide, it does something vanilla Claude doesn't: it looks at what carousel formats are currently driving engagement on the target platform. That means checking the hook structures converting right now, the slide count patterns that are outperforming, the visual-copy balance that matches current platform aesthetics, and the save-trigger mechanics that are working this week rather than last year.

This matters because carousel format isn't universal. A LinkedIn thought-leadership carousel has completely different structural logic from an Instagram educational carousel — different hook style, different information density per slide, different relationship between text and implied visual space, different CTA mechanics at the close. A skill that doesn't distinguish between them produces output that's optimised for neither.

The difference between a carousel that gets saved and one that gets scrolled past is rarely the quality of the idea — it's whether the slide architecture matches the platform's current content grammar.

The skill also calibrates to your specific context before generating. It asks three questions — about your niche, your target platform, and the core idea you want to communicate — and uses those answers to produce a slide deck that's built for your audience and your content category, not a generic template dressed up with your topic.
