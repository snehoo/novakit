The Wedding Speech That Sounds Like You, Not Like a Template

Most wedding speeches fail not because the speaker can't speak — but because the speech was written for a generic occasion rather than for the specific person being celebrated. Here's what changes when the speech is written for the actual relationship.

The speech begins. You can tell within thirty seconds. The speaker stands, the room quiets, and the first sentence lands — "When [Name] asked me to be his best man, I was honoured..." — and something settles in the room that is not disappointment exactly, but recognition. This speech is going to be like the other speeches. It will have the three stories in roughly the right order. It will have the joke that isn't quite as funny as the speaker hoped. It will have the emotional turn that was planned and therefore doesn't quite land. It will end with a toast that the room echoes back without quite having been moved.

The speaker is not the problem. They're nervous and they care and they wanted to get this right. The problem is that the speech was written the way most wedding speeches are written: by looking at what a wedding speech is supposed to contain and filling in the blanks. The specific person being celebrated — the specific thing only this speaker knows about the groom or the bride or the couple — got smoothed out in the process of producing something that looked like a wedding speech.

The speech that lands is specific in a way that couldn't be reused for anyone else. Everything else is structure around that specificity.

Why Do Most Wedding Speeches Sound Generic?

Generic speeches are produced by starting with the format rather than the relationship. Most speakers begin by researching what a best man speech or a father-of-the-bride speech is supposed to include — the welcome, the story, the acknowledgement, the toast — and then populate those sections with material from their actual relationship. The format comes first, the person comes second, and the result is a speech that could have been given with minor adjustments at any wedding for any roughly similar couple.

💡
The core problem

A wedding speech written from a template is written for a wedding. A speech written from a specific memory, in the speaker's actual voice, about the specific quality that makes this person worth celebrating — that's written for the person. Guests can feel the difference before the speaker reaches the second sentence.

The same problem appears in AI-generated speeches but worse. Vanilla AI has absorbed thousands of wedding speeches and produces a confident, warm, structurally correct version of the average of all of them. It has the right shape. It hits the expected emotional notes at the expected moments. It is completely interchangeable with every other AI-generated wedding speech. The speaker who reads it aloud knows it isn't theirs — and that awareness comes through in the delivery even when the words are right.

What Are the Structural Rules Behind a Speech That Lands?

A wedding speech that works follows six structural principles — most of which push against the instincts of first-time speech writers.

1
One story, fully told, beats three stories half-told
Most speakers pack too many stories into a speech because they're worried about leaving out something important. The result is a sequence of beginnings that never accumulate into anything. One story with physical detail — a specific place, a specific thing someone said, a specific moment with a specific consequence — is more memorable than three stories told at summary speed. Choose the story that most captures who this person is. Tell it properly. Leave the other two out.
2
The emotional turn works only if it's earned, not announced
"I'm going to get a bit emotional here" is a sentence that prevents the audience from getting emotional. It signals that a planned emotional moment is arriving, which makes the audience a witness rather than a participant. The genuine emotional moment in a speech comes from a specific detail that unexpectedly carries weight — not from the speaker telling the room to feel something. Write the detail. Trust it to do the work. Remove the announcement.
3
The close should land on something true, not something expected
Most wedding speeches end with a variation of "please raise your glasses to the happy couple." This is correct and forgettable. A close that lands says something specific about what you believe is true about this couple — one sentence that captures why this particular union makes sense, in the speaker's own terms — before the toast. The audience rises for the toast already moved, rather than politely following the cue.
4
Three to five minutes is the target, not the ceiling
The speeches people remember are almost never the longest ones. At 130 words per minute, three minutes is 390 words and five minutes is 650 words — a tight constraint that forces the speaker to cut everything that isn't doing essential work. The stories that don't make the cut in a five-minute speech are usually the ones that shouldn't have been in any version of it. Length is not depth.
5
Speak in your own register, not wedding register
Wedding speeches attract a formal vocabulary that most speakers don't use in any other context: "I am delighted to be here today," "it is a privilege to count [Name] among my closest friends," "I have no doubt that they will face life's challenges together." The speech should sound like the speaker talking, not like the speaker performing a speech. If the speaker would never say "I am delighted to be here" in any other situation, the speech shouldn't say it either. Voice calibration is what makes a speech sound like the person giving it.
6
Welcome the new partner as if you mean it, not as if it's required
Every best man speech has a line welcoming the new partner to the family or the friend group. Most of them are obligatory rather than genuine — the speaker covers it because it's expected rather than because they have something real to say. One specific observation about what the new partner has brought to the speaker's friend's life — something that shows the speaker has actually paid attention — is worth more than a paragraph of warm generic welcome.

What Does Voice Calibration Change About the AI Draft?

Voice calibration changes the speech from a correctly structured document into something that sounds like a specific person speaking about a specific relationship. Before the Event Speech Writer skill writes a word, it asks the speaker three questions: how they naturally talk (formal, casual, dry humour, openly emotional), what specific memory or quality they most want the speech to centre on, and what they want the couple or person being celebrated to feel when the speech ends. From those answers, it builds a voice profile that shapes every sentence.

Tom once drove four hours to help me move a sofa. Not four hours there and back — four hours each way, in a van he borrowed from his dad that smelled like paint and had no working radio. He didn't make a big thing of it. He just showed up at eight in the morning with a bacon sandwich.

That's Tom. And the reason I'm telling you this is that it took me fifteen years to realise that Sarah is the first person who actually deserves that version of him — because she does the same thing back.

The generic opening describes Tom correctly — loyal, kind, genuine, deserving of happiness. The calibrated opening shows one thing Tom did that proves all of it without saying any of it. By the time the speaker reaches "that's Tom," the room already knows who Tom is in a way they didn't thirty seconds ago. The transition to Sarah in the final line is earned by the story rather than announced by the speaker. That difference is entirely in the specificity of the input — the specific memory, the specific detail — and the skill's calibration to a speaker who tells stories rather than makes declarations.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should a wedding speech be?
Three to five minutes for most wedding speeches — 390 to 650 words at a natural speaking pace of 130 words per minute. The speeches that overstay their welcome are almost always the ones that haven't been cut ruthlessly enough, not the ones that ran short. If you've written eight minutes of material, the speech needs editing, not a longer slot. The skill targets three to five minutes by default and calibrates length to the event context.
What makes a wedding speech memorable?
One specific story told with physical detail, a moment of genuine emotion that isn't announced, and a close that lands on something true rather than something expected. Memorable speeches are specific to the person being celebrated in a way that couldn't be reused for anyone else. The specific detail — the thing only this speaker would know about this person — is what guests carry away. Generic warmth is forgotten before the dessert.
Should a wedding speech be memorised or read?
Neither extreme is ideal. Fully memorised speeches risk visible panic if the speaker loses their place under the pressure of a room full of people. Fully read speeches lose eye contact and feel disconnected from the audience. The best approach is a well-structured script the speaker knows well enough to look up from — reading the complex sentences, speaking the emotional moments, recovering naturally if something goes differently than rehearsed.
Can AI write a wedding speech that doesn't sound like AI wrote it?
Yes — with voice calibration. Vanilla AI produces speeches in a generic register that sounds like every other AI-generated wedding speech: warm, structured, and completely interchangeable. A skill that asks about the speaker's relationship, natural speaking style, and a specific memory they want to centre produces a speech calibrated to that speaker's voice — indistinguishable from something they wrote themselves, without the hours of staring at a blank page.
What should a best man speech include?
One story that captures the groom's character — ideally one that's funny without being embarrassing, or embarrassing in a way the groom has already laughed about. A genuine acknowledgement of what the relationship has changed, and why it's right. A welcome to the partner that feels earned rather than obligatory — one specific observation rather than a warm generic sentence. And a toast that the speaker means rather than one they've arrived at by convention.
What is the biggest mistake people make when writing wedding speeches?
Writing for the occasion rather than for the person. A speech that could have been given at any wedding for any roughly similar couple says nothing real — the specific person being celebrated could be swapped out and the speech would still work. The specific detail, the thing only this speaker knows about this person, is what makes a speech land. Most speakers bury it or leave it out entirely because they're trying to cover all the expected topics rather than say one true thing well.
