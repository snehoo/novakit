Prospect deleted your last three cold emails in under two seconds. The problem wasn't the writing. It was that the email was written without knowing anything about what's happening in their world right now.

There's a specific kind of cold email that gets deleted before the second sentence. You've received hundreds of them, and you've probably sent some too. It starts with your name used in a way that feels automated rather than familiar. The opening line references something generic about your industry or role. The value proposition arrives in the third sentence and could apply to approximately four thousand other people at companies of roughly your size. The CTA asks for thirty minutes of your time. The whole thing reads like someone filled in the blanks of a template and called it personalisation.

The uncomfortable truth for anyone who has tried to fix this with AI is that vanilla Claude produces exactly this kind of email by default. Not because the model can't write — it can write clean, grammatical, well-structured prose. But because it's writing without signal. It doesn't know that your prospect just raised a Series B three weeks ago and is actively building out their sales infrastructure. It doesn't know they posted on LinkedIn last Tuesday about a specific hiring challenge that your product directly addresses. It doesn't know the company recently expanded into a new market where your solution has a track record they can't claim yet. Without that context, the model produces the template — because the template is all it has to work with.

The Cold Outreach Email skill is built around a different starting point: research the signal first, then write the email from it.

Why Signal-Blind Outreach Gets Deleted in Under Two Seconds

Recipients of cold email have become extraordinarily fast at identifying effort level. They can read the first line of an email and know within a sentence whether the sender has looked at anything about them specifically — a recent post, a company announcement, a job opening that signals a strategic priority, a product launch — or whether they've taken a keyword from your job title and called it research. The latter gets deleted. Not because people are rude, but because their attention is worth more than the five seconds it takes to confirm there's nothing there for them.

The failure of AI-generated cold email isn't that it sounds like AI. It's that it sounds like no one. It sounds like an email that could have been sent to anyone in the same role at any company of the same size. That fungibility is the tell. Genuine outreach is specific in a way that only happens when the sender has actually looked — and the recipient knows in approximately one sentence whether that's true.

💡
The core problem

A cold email written without a live trigger — a funding round, a leadership hire, a product launch, a public statement — has no reason to land today rather than six months ago. If an email could have been sent at any time, it signals that it was sent at no particular time, for no particular reason. Prospects feel this. They just can't always articulate why they're deleting it.

The other failure is role miscalibration. An email written for a generic "Head of Marketing" reads differently to an actual Head of Marketing at a 40-person SaaS company who is one of three people on the marketing team and does their own paid acquisition than it does to a Head of Marketing at a 400-person company managing a team of twelve. Same title, completely different context, completely different concerns. An email that doesn't account for that distinction is making a guess about the recipient's actual situation — and guesses read as indifference.

What Live Signal Research Changes About the Email

Before the Cold Outreach Email skill writes a single word, it researches what's happening with the target company and role right now. That research feeds directly into what the email opens with — not a compliment, not a generic industry observation, but a specific, datable thing that happened in the prospect's world that gives the email a reason to exist today. A funding announcement that implies a specific operational challenge. A job posting that reveals a strategic gap. A LinkedIn post where the prospect named a problem out loud. A product update that created a new integration need.

This is the structural difference between an email that gets read and one that gets deleted. The opening line is not "I came across your profile and was impressed by your work at Acme." It's "Saw the Series B announcement last month — scaling the revenue org that fast usually surfaces the same three operational gaps. We help companies like yours close two of them before they become hiring problems." That email has a timestamp. It has a reason to arrive now. It costs the prospect almost nothing to keep reading, because the first line already demonstrated that the sender had looked.

The email that gets a reply is the one the prospect was already expecting someone to send — they just didn't know it until it arrived.

The skill also calibrates to role and seniority before generating the copy. A VP-level email is written differently than an ops-manager email — shorter, more direct, with the value framing aimed at strategic outcomes rather than workflow specifics. A founder email at a 15-person company gets different copy than the same title at a 200-person company, because the concern structure and the decision-making context are completely different. Same skill, genuinely different output — because the calibration happens before the writing, not as an afterthought.
