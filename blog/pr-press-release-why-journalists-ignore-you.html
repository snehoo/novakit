Why Journalists Ignore Your Press Release — and the One Structural Fix That Changes It

The news is real. The writing is professional. The wire service confirmed delivery. And nothing ran. Understanding exactly why journalists file most press releases without responding — and what the ones that do get coverage have in common — is the only useful place to start.

A journalist covering enterprise software receives somewhere between eighty and two hundred press releases a week. They file stories on three or four of them. The rest get deleted, sometimes within thirty seconds of opening. The ones that survive aren't always the most newsworthy — they're the ones that make the journalist's job easier. The ones that arrive already shaped into a story their readers would care about, with the hook visible in the first sentence, the context in the second paragraph, and a quote they can actually use.

Most founders who've sent a press release and heard nothing back assume the problem was reach — the wrong distribution list, the wrong wire service, too small an audience. Reach is almost never the problem. A journalist who covers your beat receives your release. They open it. The question is whether what they find in the first three sentences gives them a reason to keep reading. And the structural reason most releases get closed immediately has nothing to do with the quality of the news — it's that the release is written to answer the company's questions, not the journalist's.

The company's question is: how do we announce this? The journalist's question is: why would my readers care about this right now? A release that answers the first question without addressing the second is an announcement dressed in press release formatting. It tells a journalist what happened. It doesn't tell them what to write.

The Four Signals That Get a Press Release Closed in Thirty Seconds

Journalists who read a lot of press releases develop pattern recognition quickly. There are four specific signals in the first paragraph that reliably predict whether a release has a story in it — and most self-written releases trigger at least two of them before the end of the first sentence.

The company name in the headline subject position. "Acme Corp Announces Launch of New Platform" opens by telling the journalist who sent the release, not why they should care. Headlines that start with a company name signal immediately that the release was written from inside the company — where the most important fact is the company doing the thing, not the thing itself or its significance. A release written for a journalist opens with the news or the context, not the sender.

"Excited to announce" in the lead or quote. This phrase appears in approximately every press release written without PR experience. It tells the journalist that the quote was written by someone in the building who had no guidance on what a journalist needs from a quote. A usable quote contains an opinion, a market observation, or a specific claim that couldn't have appeared in the headline. "We're excited to announce" contains none of these things. A journalist who sees it in the first quote knows immediately that the release will require significant work to extract anything usable — and most don't do that work.

No connection to anything the journalist has been covering. A beat journalist is building a narrative across their stories — a set of themes, trends, and developments they return to regularly. A release that arrives with no connection to those themes is a cold pitch from an unknown company about a topic they haven't established is relevant to their readers. Releases that get picked up almost always connect explicitly to something the journalist has covered recently: a trend they've written about, a market development they've tracked, a question they've raised that the news answers.

A market size claim in the first paragraph. "$47 billion industry" or "rapidly growing market" in the lead is a reliable indicator that the release is padding for substance. Journalists understand market sizing; they also understand that anyone can pull a market size figure from a research report. A release that opens with a market size claim rather than a specific, timely news hook is signalling that the actual news isn't compelling enough to stand on its own — which may or may not be true, but the signal itself damages credibility before the journalist reaches the facts.

💡
The core problem

Every one of these signals has the same root cause: the release was written for the company, not the journalist. Fixing this isn't about better writing — it's about writing from a different starting position entirely.

What Journalists Are Actually Looking For

A journalist covering a beat has a specific editorial job: produce a set of stories each week that keep their readers informed about developments in a space they care about. Every press release they receive is evaluated against a single question — does this give me material for a story my readers would find useful or interesting? Not "is this company doing something?" but "is this a data point in a story my readers are following?"

That framing matters because it changes what needs to be in the release. The company's funding round, product launch, or partnership is only meaningful to a journalist if it connects to something larger. A $3M seed round in a vertical SaaS company is not, by itself, a story for most publications. The same round positioned as evidence of a specific funding trend in that vertical — something the journalist has been writing about, or a trend their readers are actively watching — is a data point in a story they might already be building. The news is the same. The frame is different. And the frame is what determines whether the release gets forwarded to an editor or deleted.

A press release earns coverage when it does the journalist's beat research for them — arriving already shaped into the story their readers are waiting for, sourced from the company making news.

This is why beat research matters before writing a single word of the release. Understanding what the target journalist has been covering recently — which themes they've returned to, which market questions they've raised, which angles they haven't addressed yet — gives you the frame the release needs to be built around. A release written without that research can only guess at what the journalist cares about. One written with it can position the company's news as the next chapter in a story the journalist is already invested in.

The Anatomy of a Release That Gets Read Past the First Paragraph

The structural difference between a release that gets deleted and one that gets read all the way through isn't word count or writing quality. It's the sequence of information — what appears first, what appears second, and what the journalist finds when they reach the quote.

Release element Company-first structure Beat-first structure
Headline "[Company] Announces [Product/Milestone]" "[Market context or trend] — [Company] [specific development]"
Lead sentence Names the company and the announcement Names the market situation that makes the announcement significant
Second paragraph Product or announcement detail How the announcement connects to what the journalist has been covering
CEO quote "We're thrilled to announce..." — restates the headline A specific market observation or claim that couldn't appear in a headline
Supporting context Company background and product features Market data, customer signal, or competitive context that supports the trend frame
What the journalist does next Closes it or files for background reference Forwards to an editor or replies to ask a follow-up question

The beat-first structure doesn't require more information than the company-first one. It requires the same facts arranged differently — starting with why the news matters to the journalist's readers rather than what the company did, and ending with a quote that gives the journalist something to build a paragraph around. That reordering is the entire fix. It's not a longer release or a better-written one. It's a release that answers the journalist's question before they've had to ask it.

The Quote Problem — and Why It's More Fixable Than It Looks

The CEO quote is the part of the press release that most clearly reveals whether the release was written from the inside or for the journalist. A company-first quote restates what just appeared in the headline: "We're excited to launch this product because it helps our customers achieve their goals." A journalist reads this and knows two things: the quote was written by someone who doesn't understand what a quote is for, and the release will require more work than it's probably worth.

A journalist-ready quote does one specific thing: it gives the journalist a sentence they couldn't have written themselves from the facts in the release. That means it contains an opinion ("the market has fundamentally changed in the last twelve months"), a specific observation with a concrete detail ("three of our first ten customers came from a competitor they'd been with for four years"), or a prediction with enough specificity to be quotable ("we expect the regulatory deadline to move more budget in six months than the voluntary commitments moved in three years"). Any of these give the journalist material. The generic excited-to-announce quote gives them nothing they can use.

The good news is that most founders, when asked what they actually think about their market, produce something closer to the journalist-ready version than the corporate one. The problem isn't that they don't have useful opinions — it's that press release conventions suppress those opinions in favour of language that sounds official. The skill asks for the raw version — what the spokesperson would actually say if a journalist called them — and shapes it into something that reads like a person rather than a PR office.

What Changes When the Release Is Written for the Beat

The before/after below covers the same product launch announcement — a new compliance feature for an HR software platform. One is written from inside the company. The other is written for an HR tech reporter who has spent the past several months covering the compliance burden on mid-market companies.

Written from inside — company-first
Headline: PeopleStack Launches Automated Compliance Suite to Help HR Teams Stay Ahead of Regulatory Changes

Lead: PeopleStack, a leading provider of HR management software, today announced the launch of its Automated Compliance Suite, a new module designed to help HR teams navigate the increasingly complex regulatory landscape.

Quote: "We're thrilled to bring this solution to market. Our customers have been asking for a better way to manage compliance, and we're confident this will make a real difference for HR teams everywhere." — CEO, PeopleStack

Journalist's reaction: announces a product. Every sentence could have been written without speaking to a single customer. Nothing connects this to what the journalist has been covering or why their readers would care today versus six months ago.
✓ Written for the beat — context-first
Headline: As State-Level Employment Laws Proliferate, PeopleStack Automates the Compliance Tracking That's Breaking Mid-Market HR Teams

Lead: Mid-market HR teams are now tracking an average of 14 distinct state-level employment law updates per quarter — a figure that has nearly doubled since 2022 — without dedicated compliance staff or the budget for specialist tools. PeopleStack's new Automated Compliance Suite maps regulatory changes directly to affected employee records and generates required policy updates, reducing what currently takes HR generalists two to three days per quarter to under two hours.

Quote: "The compliance burden has stopped being a legal problem and become an operations problem. Our customers aren't failing to understand the regulations — they're running out of hours to track them across thirteen states simultaneously." — CEO, PeopleStack

Journalist's reaction: the lead gives specific context a beat reporter covering HR operations will recognise. The quote offers a market observation worth pulling into a broader piece. This is fileable as a trend story, not just a product announcement.

The right column contains one invented figure — the "14 distinct state-level updates per quarter" — which in a real release would come from actual customer data or a cited research source. The structural point stands regardless: the beat-first release opens with the market problem a specific journalist has been writing about, quantifies it in terms that give the piece something to anchor to, and positions the product as the operational solution rather than the headline event. The journalist reading it has a story. The journalist reading the left column has a filing obligation they can skip.

Who Gets the Most From Getting the Release Right

Founders with real news who've sent releases before and heard nothing back — and suspect the writing rather than the news is the problem. Early-stage marketing leads producing releases without PR agency support who need a beat-calibrated output on a short turnaround. Solopreneurs making milestone announcements to niche trade press where a generic release actively signals "small company, not worth covering."

The most valuable application is for founders who have genuinely newsworthy announcements but no background in PR and no access to someone who does. A funding announcement, a significant partnership, a product launch with real customer traction — these are all stories a journalist could file. The gap between a story a journalist could file and a release that makes them want to file it is almost entirely in the structural choices made before the first word is written: what beat to target, what that reporter has been covering, how to frame the announcement as a chapter in a story they're already building. Those choices don't require a PR agency. They require knowing what to look for and how to build the release around it.

For marketing leads at early-stage companies, the skill's value is speed without sacrifice. A targeted release for a product update or hire announcement can be produced in a single session and sent the same day — calibrated to the beat rather than generic, with a quote that actually works rather than one that needs to be replaced. The releases that don't get covered aren't always the ones that arrived too late. They're the ones that arrived correctly formatted but wrong-framed. Getting the frame right takes the same amount of time as getting it wrong. It just requires a different starting question.

The Output That Makes Coverage Possible

A beat-calibrated release opens with the market context, not the company name. It gives the journalist the news hook in the first sentence and the supporting context in the second paragraph, so they have enough to pitch the piece to their editor before they've finished reading the release. The quote contains something usable — a specific claim, a market observation, a concrete timeframe — that a journalist can pull directly into a paragraph without rewriting. The boilerplate is a plain-language company description that can be compressed into a single attribution sentence without losing accuracy.

What the release can't do is create news that isn't there. A genuinely uninteresting announcement doesn't become interesting because the release is better written — but most founders dramatically underestimate how interesting their news actually is to the right journalist on the right beat. The product launch that feels incremental to a founder who's been building it for eight months looks like a market development signal to a journalist who's been tracking the space from the outside. The problem is usually not that the news is weak. It's that the release doesn't make the connection visible. That's the gap a beat-calibrated release closes — and it closes it before the journalist opens the email, not after they've decided whether to reply.
