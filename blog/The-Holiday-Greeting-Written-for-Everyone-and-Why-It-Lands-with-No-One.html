The Holiday Greeting Written for Everyone — and Why It Lands with No One

Generic AI produces greetings that are technically warm and completely forgettable. The problem isn't the writing — it's that the skill was never told who the recipient actually is.

Somewhere in your sent folder there's a holiday message you wrote with AI help and immediately felt nothing about. It went out. It was fine. Nobody replied saying it felt hollow — but nobody replied much at all. The words were warm in the way that a hotel lobby is warm: deliberately inoffensive, clearly designed for a large number of strangers, memorable to none of them.

That's the specific failure mode of AI-assisted greetings, and it's worth naming precisely because it's so easy to miss. The message wasn't wrong. It wasn't rude. It hit every beat — warmth, occasion acknowledgement, good wishes, sign-off. It just had the texture of something assembled rather than written. The recipient could tell, even if they couldn't say why. They've received fifty versions of the same message from fifty other people who also asked AI to write their holiday greetings this year.

The problem isn't that AI can't write greetings. It's that a greeting without context is just a template with different words. What makes a greeting land — what makes someone actually pause instead of scrolling past — is that it was written for them, on this occasion, from someone who knew something about the relationship. Generic AI has no access to any of that.

What Generic Greeting Output Actually Looks Like

Ask vanilla Claude to write a Diwali greeting for a business client and it will produce something structurally correct: a mention of the festival, a line about light and new beginnings, good wishes for the year ahead, your name. It reads like a greeting card from a major stationery brand — the kind that covers the widest possible ground without landing anywhere specific. Which is exactly what it is, because Claude had no idea whether this client is a long-term partner or someone you met three months ago, whether the relationship is formal or easy, whether they'd appreciate warmth or prefer brevity.

The gap between a greeting that resonates and one that doesn't isn't vocabulary. It's not even structure. It's whether the person writing it knew anything about the situation before they started. A message that references a shared milestone — a project that wrapped well, a deal that finally closed, a year that was genuinely hard — carries weight that no amount of eloquent generic prose can replicate.

💡
The core problem

A greeting calibrated to no one in particular signals exactly that. The recipient doesn't feel recognised — they feel processed. That's the opposite of what a greeting is supposed to do.

For individuals sending one or two greetings, the stakes are low enough that a generic message gets away with it. But for small business owners sending seasonal messages to a client list, creators sending holiday notes to their community, or anyone for whom the greeting is a touchpoint in an ongoing relationship — the accumulated effect of generic output is real. After two or three seasons of identical warmth-and-good-wishes messages, the greeting stops being communication and becomes noise.

Why Context First Changes Every Line

The Festival & Holiday Greeting skill doesn't start from a blank page and reach for warmth. It starts from three questions: who is this for, what is your relationship with them, and what occasion or festival are you marking. Those three answers are not decoration. They change the structural logic of what gets written.

A Diwali message to a client you've worked with for four years, sent in the week you wrapped a big project together, is a different piece of writing than a Diwali message to a new contact you want to keep warm. Same occasion. Different context. The skill knows the difference because you told it, and it uses that information to write something that couldn't have been sent to a different recipient without editing.

The skill earns the right to write a greeting by understanding the relationship first — not by guessing at it from the occasion alone.

The live research component matters here in a way that's easy to underestimate. What's working in greeting copy right now — which tones are resonating across different festivals, what structures feel current versus formulaic, how the register of professional warmth has shifted — feeds into the output before a word is written. The skill isn't drawing on a fixed idea of what a holiday greeting should look like. It's working from what's actually connecting with people right now, calibrated to your specific relationship and occasion.
