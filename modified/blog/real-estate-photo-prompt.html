You have a mid-century modern home to list. Good bones, strong natural light, a kitchen that photographs well when you get the angle right. You need hero images before the listing goes live on Friday. You ask Claude to write a photo prompt for the main living area and it comes back with: "A bright, modern living room with large windows, natural light streaming in, comfortable furniture arranged around a central coffee table, warm and inviting atmosphere."

You paste that into Midjourney. The image it produces looks like a stock photo from a furniture catalogue — the furniture is generically stylish, the light is flat and even, the room has no character at all. Nothing about it says mid-century modern, nothing about it suggests a specific time of day or a specific mood, and nothing about it would make a buyer looking at twelve listings on a Saturday morning stop scrolling. You try again with a slightly different description. Same result.

The problem isn't the image tool. It's the brief. A real estate photo prompt is not a description of a room — it's a technical specification for a shot. The lighting direction, the focal length equivalent, the time of day, the specific staging elements that should be visible, the emotional register the image needs to land in. Generic AI doesn't know any of this because it's never been told it matters. The skill does, because researching what makes property photography work is the first thing it does before writing a word.

Why Property Photography Is a Technical Discipline, Not a Description Task

Professional real estate photographers don't walk into a property and describe what they see. They make a series of precise decisions before they pick up the camera: which corner to shoot from to maximise perceived room size, whether to use ambient light or supplemental lighting, what time of day produces the warmest light through west-facing windows, which staging elements to pull into the frame and which to remove. The resulting image doesn't just document the room — it constructs a specific version of it that photographs better than it looks in person.

An AI image tool needs the same information to produce a useful result. "Bright and inviting" is not a lighting specification. "Warm atmosphere" is not a colour temperature. "Modern furniture" is not a staging brief. The gap between a description and a technical brief is the gap between an image that looks like a stock photo and one that looks like a real property shot by someone who knew exactly what they were doing.

The core problem

An AI image tool produces what you specify. Specify a description and you get a generic room. Specify a technical brief — lens equivalent, light direction, time of day, staging detail, emotional register — and you get a shot that could go straight into a listing.

The listing tier compounds this. A $280,000 terrace house and a $2.8M waterfront property need completely different photography — different lighting moods, different staging density, different shot compositions, different emotional registers. The terrace needs images that feel attainable and warm. The waterfront needs images that feel aspirational and considered. Generic AI writes the same kind of prompt for both, because it has no awareness of price point, buyer profile, or what emotion needs to be created in the person scrolling the listing at 9pm on a Sunday.

What Live Research Changes for a Photography Prompt Skill

The Real Estate Photo Prompt skill researches current property photography trends before writing a single line of the brief. Not what was working in real estate imagery two years ago — what compositions, lighting approaches, and staging aesthetics are performing in listings at your property tier right now. The market for property photography shifts: the warm golden-hour aesthetic that dominated listings in 2022 is no longer cutting through in the same way; the staging density that luxury properties used to favour has thinned out considerably. A skill that researches before it writes knows this.

That research shapes which technical decisions get made in the brief. If twilight exterior shots are performing strongly for properties in a particular price bracket right now, the skill knows to include them and to specify them correctly — the exact blue-hour window, the interior lighting that needs to be on, the exterior elements that should be in frame. If drone perspectives are currently overused in a specific market segment and actually reducing click-through, the skill knows to steer away from them. Generic AI produces whatever it thinks real estate photography looks like from its training data. The skill produces what's working in the current market.

A photo brief written without knowledge of what's performing in listings right now produces images that look like last year's property market.

The calibration to property type is the second major differentiator. A Victorian terrace, a new-build apartment, a rural farmhouse, and a penthouse with city views each require a completely different photographic approach — different focal lengths, different light quality, different staging register, different compositional priorities. The skill asks which property type it's working with and adjusts every technical parameter accordingly. Generic AI produces a prompt that could describe any residential interior and does justice to none of them specifically.

What the Real Estate Photo Prompt Skill Produces

The skill asks four questions before writing: property type and tier, the key selling features of the specific property, the target buyer profile, and the listing context — whether this is for an online portal, a social campaign, a print brochure, or all three. Those answers determine a complete photographic brief for each shot, not just a description:

1
Shot composition and camera position
The specific corner, height, and angle that maximises perceived space for this room type. For a narrow kitchen, that means a specific wide-angle equivalent from the far corner at counter height. For a living room with a feature wall, it means a three-quarter angle that keeps the wall in frame without distorting the furniture scale. The brief specifies the position, not just the subject.
2
Lighting specification
Time of day, light direction, colour temperature, and whether the look requires supplemental fill or pure ambient. A south-facing kitchen at 10am has a specific quality of light that the brief captures in technical terms — not "bright and natural" but the actual parameters that tell an AI image tool what to render. The emotional register of the lighting is specified, not implied.
3
Staging and detail brief
Which elements should be visible and where — a single vase, an open book, a specific textile — and which should be removed from frame. Staging density is calibrated to listing tier: entry-level properties need warm, lived-in details; luxury properties need considered sparseness. The brief specifies both what to include and what to exclude, which is as important for a clean result.
4
Emotional register and buyer target
The specific feeling the image needs to create in the buyer looking at it — not "inviting" but the precise emotional state that moves a specific buyer profile from scrolling to clicking. A first-home buyer needs to feel possibility. A downsizer needs to feel ease. A luxury buyer needs to feel arrival. The brief is written toward the buyer, not toward the room.
5
Full shot list for the property
The skill doesn't produce a single prompt — it produces a complete shot list for the property, with a technical brief for each key space. Hero exterior, primary living area, kitchen, main bedroom, and any feature spaces specific to this property. Each brief is self-contained: paste it directly into an AI image tool and get a consistent result across the whole listing.
