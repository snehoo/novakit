You've used AI to write a LinkedIn post. It came back polished, well-structured, three tight paragraphs or a clean hook-and-list. You posted it. It got twelve likes — half of them from people you know — and then disappeared. If that's happened more than twice, the issue isn't your writing. It's that the AI you used had no idea what LinkedIn's feed actually rewards right now.

LinkedIn's algorithm scores posts based on signals that shift constantly: how quickly engagement arrives, what format the post uses, whether the opening line earns the "see more" click, how your specific audience tends to interact with content. None of that is in Claude's training data. Claude was trained on text from the internet at a fixed point in time. It writes competent LinkedIn posts the way a strong copywriter who hasn't used LinkedIn in two years would write them — confident in the fundamentals, completely blind to what's changed.

The LinkedIn Post Engine skill was built to close that gap. Before it writes anything, it researches what's currently working — formats, hook structures, post lengths, engagement patterns — and uses that live context to calibrate the output. The result is a post that fits the platform as it exists this week, not as it existed when the model was trained.

Why Generic AI Output Vanishes in the Feed

The specific failure mode of vanilla AI on LinkedIn isn't bad writing. The writing is usually fine. The problem is structural irrelevance — posts written to conventions the algorithm has moved past.

A classic example: the five-line hook with each sentence on its own line. That format dominated LinkedIn from roughly 2020 to 2023. It worked because it drove "see more" clicks, which were a strong early signal to the algorithm. Enough people copied it that LinkedIn's feed eventually started deprioritising it — not because LinkedIn explicitly penalised it, but because mass adoption eroded the engagement advantage. AI tools trained before or during that shift will still default to it, because it represents a reliable pattern in their training data. It looks right. It just doesn't perform.

The same happens with post length, with CTA placement, with whether a question at the end helps or hurts reach, with whether a hook built on a contrarian claim gets amplified or ignored. Each of these has a current answer that differs from the 2022 answer. A skill that only knows the 2022 answer will keep producing the 2022 post.

💡
The core problem

LinkedIn rewards posts that fit current feed mechanics — and those mechanics change faster than any AI model updates. Writing a good post to an outdated format is like sending the right message to the wrong address.

This is compounded by the calibration problem. A post that works for a B2B founder in SaaS is structurally different from one that works for a career coach writing to mid-career professionals. Not just in tone — in format, in hook type, in how personal to go, in where to place the insight vs. the story. Generic AI produces generic output because it has no way to know which of those you are. It writes to the average.

What Live Research Actually Changes

The LinkedIn Post Engine skill researches the current feed before producing a single line of copy. That means format patterns that are actually earning reach right now, not patterns that were strong eighteen months ago. It means hook structures that are driving the "see more" click in your specific content category. It means post length calibrated to what's getting seen in full rather than scrolled past.

This matters more on LinkedIn than almost any other platform because LinkedIn's algorithm is unusually sensitive to format signals relative to content quality. A well-written post in a dead format will underperform a merely decent post in a working one. The research isn't about following trends for the sake of it — it's about not wasting a genuinely good idea on a delivery mechanism the algorithm has stopped rewarding.

The skill doesn't write what sounds good. It writes what the feed will actually show.

Beyond format, the live research informs hook strategy. Different audience segments respond to different opening moves — a direct counter-intuitive claim works well with operators and practitioners, while a specific personal scenario tends to perform better with early-career professionals. The skill identifies which category your post sits in and writes the hook accordingly. That's not something you get from "write me a LinkedIn post about X."
