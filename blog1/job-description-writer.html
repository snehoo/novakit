You're hiring your first head of growth. You know exactly who you need — someone who's built acquisition from scratch before, comfortable with ambiguity, doesn't need a playbook because they're the one who writes it. You open Claude and describe the role. It comes back with a job description that lists eight years of experience, a degree requirement, proficiency in four specific tools, and a benefits section that mentions "competitive salary" and "collaborative culture."

The person you're trying to hire reads that JD and moves on. Not because they're underqualified — they have everything you actually need. But because the JD signals a company that thinks in checklists, and the person you want has already learned to skip those. The candidate who applies is the one who meets all eight requirements and is comfortable in a structured environment with an established playbook. Which is precisely not the person you described.

This is the specific failure of generic AI for job descriptions: it produces the JD that sounds complete and attracts the wrong room. The requirements list, the responsibilities list, the benefits bullet points — all assembled from the accumulated pattern of job descriptions it was trained on, with no understanding of the stage you're at, the culture you're building, or the specific kind of ambiguity tolerance you need from this particular hire.

Why a Job Description Is Employer Brand, Not a Checklist

The candidate you most want to hire is not scrolling job boards out of desperation. They're evaluating whether your company is worth their time before they apply. The job description is the first piece of writing they read from you — the first signal about how you think, what you value, and whether working there would be interesting. A generic JD with a requirements list and a boilerplate culture section tells them everything they need to know: this company hasn't thought carefully about who they want, which probably means they haven't thought carefully about a lot of other things either.

Experienced operators and strong individual contributors read job descriptions the way a founder reads a term sheet — looking for what it reveals about the other party as much as what it explicitly says. A JD that requires eight years of experience for a role that genuinely needs three is not protecting you from under-qualified applicants. It's signalling that nobody inside the company has thought carefully about what this job actually is. A JD that mentions "fast-paced environment" and "wear many hats" without saying anything specific about the actual work is signalling that the company doesn't know how to talk about what they do. Both filter out the people you want.

💡
The core problem

A job description written from a requirements checklist tells candidates what you need from them. A well-calibrated JD tells them what they'll be doing, why it matters, and whether the environment suits how they work best — which is the information that makes the right candidate apply and the wrong one self-select out.

The stage problem compounds this. A pre-seed company hiring its first sales person needs a radically different JD from a Series B company filling an SDR role. The former needs someone who will build the motion from nothing, is energised by the ambiguity of that, and understands they're joining before product-market fit is confirmed. The latter needs someone who can execute a defined playbook at volume. These are different jobs and different people — but generic AI, given the same role title, produces something that could describe either. The candidate reads it and can't tell what they're actually walking into.

What Stage-Calibrated Output Changes for Hiring

The Job Description Writer skill asks about your company stage, team size, the specific outcomes this role owns in the first 90 days, and what kind of working environment you're being honest about — not what you aspire to be, but what it's actually like to work there right now. Those answers don't just change individual phrases. They change the architecture of the whole document.

A seed-stage JD written by this skill leads with the problem the role solves and the specific opportunity it represents — because the candidate you want at seed is evaluating whether the problem is interesting, not whether the benefits package is competitive. A Series A JD calibrates to a candidate who wants to know that there's enough structure to be effective but enough autonomy to grow. The skill researches what's working in JD formats right now — what language is cutting through, what phrasing signals the kind of culture that attracts strong operators, what requirements language actively repels the people you need.

The right candidate isn't reading your JD to decide if they qualify. They're reading it to decide if you're worth their time.

The requirements calibration is where the practical difference is clearest. Generic AI defaults to the requirements it has seen most often for a given role title — which skews heavily toward large-company JDs because those companies post the most jobs. A startup hiring its first product manager does not need five years of experience, a CS degree, and proficiency in Jira, Confluence, and Figma. It needs someone who has shipped product before, is comfortable making decisions with incomplete data, and can write clearly enough to align a small engineering team. Those are different requirements, and the JD that lists them attracts a different and better-suited candidate pool.

What the Job Description Writer Skill Does

The skill runs a four-question calibration before writing anything, then produces a complete JD structured around what actually makes the right candidate apply:

Stage and context calibration
Company stage, team size, funding status, and the honest description of what the working environment is like right now. 2
Outcome-led role description
Instead of a responsibilities list, the skill writes what success looks like in 30, 60, and 90 days. This tells the candidate what the job actually is — not an abstracted list of duties but a specific picture of what they'll be building and what done looks like. Strong candidates evaluate this section carefully. It's where they decide whether the role is interesting enough to pursue.
3
Requirements calibrated to what you actually need
The skill asks what the role genuinely requires versus what would be nice to have, and writes requirements that reflect the distinction. Years-of-experience requirements are replaced with capability descriptions. Degree requirements are only included when they're genuinely necessary. The result is a requirements section that describes the person you need rather than the person who attended the right school and stayed somewhere for long enough.
4
Live research on what's working in JD formats right now
Before writing, the skill researches what language and structure is attracting strong candidates in your sector at your stage — what's cutting through the noise, what phrasing signals the culture that draws experienced operators, what requirements language is actively filtering out the people you most want to reach. Not the accumulated average of all JDs ever written. What's working now, in your specific hiring context.
5
Quality pass before delivery
Boilerplate culture descriptions, requirements that don't reflect the actual role, vague outcome statements that tell candidates nothing — these get caught and rewritten automatically.
