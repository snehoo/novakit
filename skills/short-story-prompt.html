Core Premise · Dark Romance
A specific situation — not a theme.
Genre: Dark Romance / Contemporary · Length: 8,000–12,000w · Platform: Kindle Unlimited

A forensic accountant hired to audit a private equity firm discovers the irregularities trace back to the man she's been sleeping with for six weeks — a man who doesn't know what she does for a living.

The complication: He's the whistleblower, not the architect. Filing her report means destroying the only person who was trying to stop it.

Opening hook: The wire transfer was dated two days before the fire, and the account number matched the one on his phone when it buzzed at 2 a.m. and she didn't look away fast enough.
Premise contains character, want, obstacle, and decision. Hook drops into tension — no weather, no waking up.
Character Seeds
Contradiction first. Biography second.
Protagonist seed:
Core contradiction: meticulous about evidence, reckless about trust
Surface want: close the audit, protect her career
Hidden fear: that she only sees what she wants to see
Voice: clipped, ironic, deflects with competence

Opposing force seed:
What he wants: to expose the firm before it collapses a pension fund
Why he's not wrong: the people who will lose are the ones no one is protecting
Character seeds give the writer a person to inhabit — not a resume to describe.
Structure Beat Map · 7 Beats
Scene-by-scene roadmap at your word count.
Beat 1 — Opening hook (~8%): Mid-audit, she finds the transfer. We are already in motion.
Beat 2 — Status quo (~10%): Six weeks, no real names, perfect deniability. What she stands to lose is named.
Beat 3 — Inciting incident (~15%): She traces the account to him. She does not file immediately.
Beat 4 — Rising complication (~30%): She digs deeper to disprove it. Finds the whistleblower thread instead.
Beat 5 — Dark moment (~65%): Her firm is pressured to close the audit. She must choose a side by morning.
Platform note: KU readers need a hook in the first 10% or they return the book — beat 1 is non-negotiable.
Prompt Variations · 3 Takes
Same premise — three completely different stories.
Variation 1 — Genre shift: Transposed to a near-future city where financial crimes are prosecuted as public spectacles. The moral stakes become theatrical.

Variation 2 — POV shift: Told from his perspective. The reader knows she's the auditor from page one. He doesn't. Dramatic irony carries the tension.

Variation 3 — Structural inversion: Begins at the filing of the report. The story moves backward, revealing what she knew and when.
Each variation genuinely changes the reader's experience — not just the setting details.
